# **Foundations of Modern Geodesy: The Shape of the Earth, Gravity Modeling, and Relativistic Timekeeping**

Modern geodesy represents the profound intersection of geometry, geophysics, and relativistic physics. The discipline is traditionally defined by its three fundamental pillars: the measurement of the Earth's geometric shape, the orientation of the Earth in space, and the determination of the Earth's gravity field. However, in the era of satellite navigation, quantum sensing, and high-performance digital elevation modeling, a fourth pillar has emerged as the bedrock of spatial measurement: the precise determination of time. Because the speed of light perfectly binds distance and time, the spatial coordinates used to map a floodplain, navigate a satellite, or define a global vertical datum are fundamentally reliant on our ability to model relativistic time dilation and minute variations in gravitational potential.

This comprehensive report explores the theoretical and practical frameworks that define the modern geodetic sciences. The analysis traces the mathematical progression from idealized geometric models—the sphere and the ellipsoid—to the highly complex, dynamically fluctuating geoid. It examines the instruments and methodologies used to measure gravity, ranging from terrestrial quantum atom interferometers to low-earth orbit satellite missions such as the Gravity Recovery and Climate Experiment (GRACE) and the Gravity field and steady-state Ocean Circulation Explorer (GOCE). Furthermore, the report details the mathematical formalisms of spherical harmonics and Stokes' integral, which translate gravity anomalies into geoid undulations. Ultimately, the synthesis of these gravitational and chronological measurements is applied to the critical domain of Digital Elevation Models (DEMs) and the ongoing global transition to modern, gravity-based vertical datums such as the North American-Pacific Geopotential Datum of 2022 (NAPGD2022) and the International Height Reference System (IHRS).

## **The Shape of the Earth: Sphere, Ellipsoid, and Geoid**

The conceptualization of the Earth's shape has evolved through successive stages of mathematical refinement, driven by the increasing precision of measurement technologies and the demands of global navigation. While the Earth is colloquially described as a sphere—and indeed, as a first-order approximation, is often modeled as a sphere with a radius of ![][image1] kilometers—the physical reality of a rotating planetary body demands a more complex geometric framework.1 The centrifugal force generated by the Earth's rotation around its polar axis forces the planet's mass to bulge outward at the equator and flatten at the poles, necessitating the use of an ellipsoid.2

### **The Reference Ellipsoid and Spheroidal Geometry**

To establish a globally consistent coordinate system, geodesy utilizes a mathematical figure known as an Earth ellipsoid, or oblate spheroid. This three-dimensional shape is formed by rotating a two-dimensional ellipse around its minor axis.4 This geometric model closely approximates the overall size and shape of the Earth and serves as the foundational reference frame for calculating geodetic latitude, longitude, and ellipsoidal height.6

An oblate spheroid is defined primarily by two geometric parameters: the semi-major axis (![][image2]), which represents the equatorial radius from the center of the Earth to the equator, and the semi-minor axis (![][image3]), which represents the polar radius from the center of the Earth to the geographic poles.4 The difference between these two axes is slightly more than 21 kilometers, resulting in an oblateness or flattening factor of approximately 0.335%.7

Throughout the history of modern geodesy, various reference ellipsoids have been calculated to best fit the Earth's surface, either globally or regionally. Many methods exist for the determination of the axes of an Earth ellipsoid, ranging from early meridian arc measurements to modern satellite geodesy and the analysis of continental geodetic networks.7 Prominent historical examples include the Bessel ellipsoid of 1841 and the international Hayford ellipsoid of 1924\.7 In North America, the Clarke 1866 spheroid was long the standard for regional mapping.5 However, the advent of global satellite positioning required reference ellipsoids whose origin coincided precisely with the Earth's center of mass.

Today, the most universally applied models are the Geodetic Reference System 1980 (GRS80) and the World Geodetic System 1984 (WGS84).5 While fundamentally similar, slight differences exist in their defining constants, reflecting the ongoing refinement of geodetic measurements.

The GRS 1980 ellipsoid was selected as the foundational geometry for the North American Datum of 1983 (NAD83) because it did an exceptionally good job of mimicking the Earth's curvature for that specific continent.5 The WGS 1984 ellipsoid shares the identical semi-major axis but defines its semi-minor axis with a microscopic variation.5 Although the difference in the polar radius between WGS84 and GRS80 is a mere fraction of a millimeter, this distinction is vital for maintaining mathematical rigor in high-precision Global Navigation Satellite Systems (GNSS) and orbital mechanics computations. Geometric coordinates referring to the GRS80 ellipsoid or the WGS84 ellipsoid are practically identical for horizontal positioning, but geoid undulations computed against them can present discrepancies approaching 93 centimeters.8

There is also a critical distinction between a "mean" Earth ellipsoid and a "reference" Earth ellipsoid. A data set which describes the global average of the Earth's surface curvature is called the mean Earth ellipsoid.7 It refers to a theoretical coherence between the geographic latitude and the meridional curvature of the geoid, effectively creating an ideal ellipsoid that has the same total volume as the geoid.7 While the mean Earth ellipsoid is the ideal basis of global geodesy, practical regional surveying relies on locally anchored reference ellipsoids to minimize distortion over specific continents.

### **The Geoid and the Physics of Mean Sea Level**

While the reference ellipsoid provides a pristine mathematical surface for horizontal positioning, it fails to account for the heterogeneous distribution of mass within the Earth's interior.5 The Earth's crust and mantle are not uniform; the presence of dense tectonic plates, massive mountain ranges, deep oceanic trenches, and varying mantle composition results in a complex, highly irregular gravitational field.5 Consequently, a purely geometric model cannot accurately dictate the behavior of fluids. Because water flows in response to gravitational potential energy rather than geometric distance, water could theoretically flow "uphill" with respect to the smooth mathematical surface of the ellipsoid.10

To address the physics of fluid mechanics and vertical orientation, geodesy defines a purely physical model known as the geoid. The geoid is defined as the equipotential surface of the Earth's gravity field that most closely approximates global mean sea level (MSL) in the absence of dynamic oceanographic effects such as winds, ocean currents, and tidal variations.5 Because the geoid is an equipotential surface, the direction of the force of gravity is perfectly perpendicular to it at every location on the globe.5

If the Earth possessed a perfectly uniform density and behaved as a fluid in hydrostatic equilibrium, the geoid and the reference ellipsoid would perfectly coincide. However, due to the aforementioned mass anomalies, the geoid undulates constantly.5 The geoid rises over dense continental masses (where the stronger local gravitational pull effectively "piles up" the equipotential surface) and dips over mass deficits such as oceanic trenches.5 Unlike ellipsoidal models which can span the entire globe with a single equation, accurate geoid models are intensely locally based, reflecting the unique gravitational fingerprint of the underlying geology.12

## **The Mathematics of the Geopotential**

Modeling the geoid requires a rigorous mathematical description of the Earth's gravitational potential field. The total gravity potential (![][image16]) at any point in or around the Earth is the sum of two distinct physical forces: the gravitational potential due to the attraction of the Earth's mass (![][image17]) and the centrifugal potential due to the Earth's rotation (![][image18]).15

### **Spherical Harmonic Expansion**

In regions of free space outside the Earth's masses—such as the orbital paths of navigation and gravity-measuring satellites—the mass density is essentially zero. In these regions, the gravitational potential satisfies Laplace's equation (![][image19]), which is the fundamental differential equation of gravity.16 When Laplace's equation is solved in a spherical coordinate system (incorporating radial distance ![][image20], colatitude ![][image21], and longitude ![][image22]), it yields a series expansion of orthogonal mathematical functions known as spherical harmonics.16

Spherical harmonics provide a highly powerful and convenient mathematical tool to describe the Earth's complex gravitational field outside of its surface.16 Just as a Fourier series deconstructs a complex, jagged acoustic wave into a series of simple sine and cosine waves, a spherical harmonic series deconstructs the three-dimensional gravitational potential into a linear combination of basis functions spanning the globe.3 Because the Earth is nearly spherical, a spherical harmonic series expansion captures the actual gravity field with increasing fidelity as more terms are added to the summation.3

The standard expression for the Earth's gravitational potential using spherical harmonics involves the summation of associated Legendre functions, which are quantified by two integers: their "degree" (![][image23] or ![][image24]) and their "order" (![][image25]).1 The degree of the harmonic dictates the spatial wavelength, effectively defining the horizontal resolution of the gravity field features being modeled. Low-degree harmonics represent massive, global-scale variations in mass distribution. For example, the most significant and largest spherical harmonic term is the second-degree zonal harmonic (![][image26]), which mathematically accounts for the Earth's oblateness.17 High-degree harmonics capture highly localized, short-wavelength topographical features such as individual mountain peaks or oceanic trenches.17

### **Stokes' Integral and the Challenge of Truncation Errors**

While spherical harmonics provide the overarching global mathematical framework, translating discrete surface gravity measurements into an actual model of geoid undulations relies on Stokes' Integral, a foundational formula published by George Gabriel Stokes in 1849\.14 Stokes' theorem is a cornerstone of physical geodesy; it provides the explicit analytical relationship between the continuous surface integral of gravity anomalies (![][image52]) over the entire globe and the resulting geoidal height (![][image12]) at any specific computation point.14

The classical Stokes formula relies on an error kernel known as the Stokes function, which assigns mathematical weights to the influence of gravity anomalies based on their spherical distance from the computation point. However, the major drawback of Stokes's formula is its strict requirement for absolute global coverage.14 The integral demands gravity anomaly data spanning the entire Earth to compute a perfectly accurate geoid height at a single point.

Because achieving continuous, high-density global coverage of terrestrial gravity data is impossible—particularly over vast oceans, dense jungles, and politically restricted territories—practitioners are forced to limit the area of mathematical integration to a localized spherical cap with a defined radius (![][image53]) surrounding the computation point.14

This localized restriction introduces significant mathematical vulnerabilities. Truncating the integral to a regional cap incurs what is known as a truncation error, effectively ignoring the gravitational influence of remote zones located outside the spherical cap.22 Numerical evaluations of Poisson's integral theorem and Stokes' derivations have demonstrated that tremendous truncation errors occur unless the integration is extended globally.22 The lack of gravity data coverage over very large parts of the globe causes such significant errors in the calculation of geoidal heights and vertical extensions of anomalous gravity that, in unmodified forms, the results can be rendered virtually meaningless.22 Furthermore, paradoxically, increasing the size of the integrated cap does not always decrease the root-mean-square (RMS) error of the geoid undulation, due to the complex zeroes of the Stokes kernel.23

To mitigate these profound mathematical challenges, modern geodesy heavily employs a "remove-compute-restore" technique combined with modified Stokes kernels (such as those pioneered by Meissl, Wong and Gore, and others).14 This technique functions analogously to applying a window function in spectral analysis.23 First, a low-degree global Earth Gravity Model (EGM) derived from satellite tracking is utilized to compute the long-to-medium wavelength components of the geoid.14 These known long-wavelength effects are mathematically "removed" from the local terrestrial gravity anomaly data. Next, the localized, short-wavelength contributions of the geoid are "computed" from the residual terrestrial gravity information using a modified Stokes' integral over the localized spherical cap.14 Finally, the long-wavelength components from the global model are "restored" to produce a highly detailed, seamless geoid model.14 This synergistic approach harnesses the strengths of both global satellite tracking and local high-density terrestrial surveying.

## **Measuring Gravity: From Terrestrial Instruments to Satellite Constellations**

The accurate realization of the geoid and the execution of Stokes' integral require highly precise, globally distributed measurements of gravitational acceleration (![][image54]).25 The acceleration of an object under the influence of the Earth's gravity varies significantly across the planetary surface, ranging from approximately ![][image55] at the equator to about ![][image56] at the poles.25 This baseline variance is driven by the Earth's oblateness—because the poles are physically closer to the Earth's center of mass—and the fact that centrifugal acceleration due to rotation is maximum at the equator and zero at the poles.2

### **Terrestrial, Airborne, and Marine Gravimetry**

Terrestrial gravity measurements form the high-resolution foundation of physical geodesy. These measurements are fundamentally classified into two categories: absolute and relative gravimetry.25

**Absolute Gravimeters** determine the exact magnitude of gravitational acceleration at a specific point in time and space.25 Because their internal length and time measurements are tied directly to traceable metrological standards, they output the full-field gravity value (e.g., ![][image61]).25 Classical absolute gravimeters operate using Laser Interferometry (LI).26 These highly sensitive instruments track the trajectory of a macroscopic test mass—typically a precisely machined corner-cube reflector—as it is dropped in a vacuum chamber, directly measuring its acceleration via the interference fringes of a laser beam.25 Modern classical instruments can measure ![][image54] with an accuracy of about ![][image62], or one part per billion.25

**Relative Gravimeters**, conversely, do not measure the full-field gravity value. Instead, they measure the spatial difference in gravity between two distinct locations or the temporal change in gravity over time at a fixed location.25 Classical relative gravimeters rely on highly calibrated mechanical mass-spring systems.26 In these systems, the elongation of a specialized spring balances the weight of a test mass; as the gravitational acceleration changes, the weight changes, and the varying elongation is measured.26 Superconducting gravimeters represent the pinnacle of stationary relative measurement, while Micro-Electromechanical-System (MEMS) gravimeters offer small, mass-producible alternatives for borehole measurements and sensor networks.26 Relative gravimeters are highly portable and form the backbone of airborne (e.g., the GT-2A system) and shipborne marine (e.g., the SEA III system) gravity campaigns, which map regions like mountain ranges and oceans where static ground measurements are impossible.25 However, the primary limitation of classical mechanical relative gravimeters is instrumental drift over time, requiring them to be frequently calibrated against absolute reference stations.26

A profound technological paradigm shift is currently occurring with the advent of **Quantum Gravimetry**, specifically instruments based on Atom Interferometers (AIs).25 Rather than using a macroscopic mechanical spring or a falling mirror, quantum gravimeters use laser-cooled atoms—frequently Cesium or Rubidium—as the free-falling test mass.25 By exploiting the wave-particle duality of matter through Raman transitions, atom interferometers measure the phase shift of the atomic matter-waves as they are exposed to the local gravitational field.26

Quantum atomic gravimeters offer extraordinary advantages over classical mechanical systems. Most notably, they feature "zero drift" and immense long-term stability, making them unparalleled for continuous, long-duration measurements in harsh dynamic environments.26

### **Satellite Gravimetry: The GRACE and GOCE Missions**

While terrestrial, marine, and airborne gravimetry provide excellent short-wavelength detail, they suffer from vast geographical data gaps. It is logistically and economically unfeasible to survey the entirety of the deep oceans, the polar ice caps, and inaccessible mountain ranges with aircraft or ships.22 To map the global geopotential accurately and uniformly, the geodetic community relies on dedicated low-Earth orbit (LEO) satellite gravimetry missions.

**GRACE (Gravity Recovery and Climate Experiment):** Launched in 2002 and succeeded by the GRACE Follow-On (GRACE-FO) mission, this revolutionary program utilized the measurement principle of Satellite-to-Satellite Tracking in the Low-Low mode (SST-LL).28 The configuration involved two identical LEO satellites placed in the exact same orbit, separated by several hundred kilometers.28 The two satellites acted together as a single, highly distributed instrument.

As the lead satellite passed over an area of mass inhomogeneity—such as an unusually dense geological structure or a massive mountain range—the localized increase in gravitational pull accelerated it away from the trailing satellite. The microscopic variations in the range distance between the two satellites were continuously measured with the highest possible accuracy using a high-accuracy K-band microwave link (and laser-ranging interferometry in the GRACE-FO mission).28 Precise onboard accelerometers were utilized to measure and filter out non-gravitational effects such as atmospheric drag and solar radiation pressure.28

GRACE's primary breakthrough was not just spatial, but temporal. By producing monthly gravity field models, GRACE allowed geodesists, climatologists, and hydrologists to observe the time-varying gravity field of the Earth.21 It accurately monitored seasonal mass transport phenomena, effectively "weighing" the shifting mass of the Earth's water systems from space. This provided unique, direct observations of the water cycle in massive river basins, the rapid melting of the Greenland and Antarctic ice sheets, associated sea-level changes, and the alarming depletion of major underground aquifers.28

**GOCE (Gravity field and steady-state Ocean Circulation Explorer):** Operating from 2009 until it ran out of fuel in October 2013, the European Space Agency's GOCE mission was designed with a different objective: to determine the stationary, static gravitational field to an unprecedented degree of spatial resolution and accuracy.9

Unlike GRACE's twin-satellite SST-LL method, GOCE utilized the principle of Satellite Gravity Gradiometry (SGG).28 The spacecraft's primary payload was the Electrostatic Gravity Gradiometer (EGG), which consisted of an ensemble of ultra-sensitive test masses (accelerometers) mounted precisely near the center of mass of the single satellite.9 By directly measuring acceleration differences in all three spatial dimensions within the satellite itself, the gradiometer captured signals representing the second derivatives of the gravitational potential.9 A massive advantage of this methodology is that non-gravitational accelerations are the same for all measurements inside the satellite and therefore vanish perfectly by mathematical differentiation, leaving a pure, unadulterated gravitational signal.28

To maximize sensitivity to the Earth's small-scale mass inhomogeneities—such as ocean ridges, valleys, and mantle structures—GOCE was placed in a sun-synchronous orbit at an exceptionally low average altitude of 250 to 270 kilometers.9 At this extreme low-Earth orbit, atmospheric drag is significant and would normally cause rapid orbital decay, severely corrupting the delicate gravity measurements. To counteract this, GOCE was built with a highly aerodynamic, octagonal prism shape to minimize its drag profile.9 More importantly, it employed a complex Drag-Free and Attitude Control System (DFACS) powered by an electric Ion Propulsion Assembly.9 This closed-loop control system utilized acceleration measurements from the science data to provide continuous, real-time thrust from ion engines, exactly counteracting the air drag and creating a perfect "drag-free" free-fall environment for the gradiometer.9

The resulting global gravity models derived from GOCE were spectacular, offering accuracies at the 2-centimeter level for geoid heights at a high spatial resolution of 80 to 100 kilometers (corresponding to spherical harmonic degrees of 200 and above).28 This data improved the accuracy of the static gravity field by a factor of at least 100 compared to older models.28 When combined with GRACE temporal data and terrestrial gravity anomalies, these satellite missions form the definitive backbone of modern high-resolution geopotential models like GGM05C and the EGM series.21

## **Gravity Anomalies and Topographic Corrections**

When raw gravity measurements are collected—whether by a quantum gravimeter on a ship or extracted from a satellite model—they must be mathematically reduced and standardized to interpret the underlying geology and to properly execute Stokes' integral for geoid modeling. This standardization involves calculating gravity anomalies, which represent the difference between the actual observed gravity at a physical point and the theoretical gravity predicted by the reference ellipsoid for that specific latitude.22

The primary metric utilized in broad-scale physical geodesy is the **Free-Air Anomaly**.30 This anomaly corrects the observed gravity measurement purely for the elevation of the measurement station above the reference ellipsoid (or mean sea level).30 Because gravity decreases in proportion to the square of the distance from the Earth's center of mass, physically elevating a sensor decreases the observed gravity reading. The free-air correction algebraically strips away this height factor, standardizing all readings to a common datum level.30 Crucially, the free-air anomaly does *not* take into account the physical mass of the terrain located between the sensor and the reference ellipsoid.31

Because it retains the gravitational signal of the topography, the free-air anomaly is highly representative of the actual geological mass distribution.32 It is directly proportional to the thickness and density contrast of subsurface bodies, making it exceptionally useful for structural modeling.32 In marine geodesy and offshore exploration, where there is no physical rock terrain situated above the datum level, free-air anomalies are heavily utilized to map sub-seafloor basins and structures.32 Furthermore, when mapping the global geoid, the free-air anomaly is the specific functional input required by Stokes' integral to compute geoid undulations.19

However, in terrestrial environments, the physical mass of the topography itself (e.g., mountains, plateaus) exerts a significant, localized gravitational pull that can mask deeper geological structures. To account for this overlying mass, geophysicists calculate the **Bouguer Anomaly**.30

The Bouguer reduction applies a rigorous mathematical correction to the free-air anomaly to effectively remove the gravitational attraction of the rock layer sitting between the observation point and the reference ellipsoid.31 In its simplest form, it models this terrain as an infinite, flat horizontal plate of rock (the Bouguer plate).31 Assuming a standard average density (![][image67]) for crustal rock of ![][image68], the standard Bouguer plate correction is mathematically defined as ![][image69].31 When the standard density is applied, this simplifies to a correction factor of approximately ![][image70] of elevation (![][image10]).31 More advanced complete Bouguer anomalies incorporate detailed variable topographic density and rigorous terrain models to account for the jaggedness of mountains and valleys beyond a simple flat plate.34

By systematically stripping away both the effects of elevation and the gravitational pull of the topographic mass, the Bouguer anomaly successfully isolates gravitational variations caused strictly by density differences deep within the Earth's crust and upper mantle.30 The interpretation of these anomalies is a cornerstone of geophysics. For instance, it is highly common to observe positive free-air anomalies over massive mountain ranges (due to the sheer volume of rock pulling down on the meter) but highly *negative* Bouguer anomalies in the exact same location.30 Once the visible mountain mass is mathematically removed by the Bouguer correction, the remaining negative anomaly reveals the presence of deep, low-density crustal roots displacing denser mantle material below the mountains. This classic geodetic observation serves as the primary evidence for the principle of isostatic compensation, proving that mountains "float" on the mantle much like icebergs in the ocean.30

## **Measuring Time: Relativistic Effects and Precision Timekeeping**

While geometry and gravity dictate the theoretical structure of the geodetic reference frame, the actual practical measurement of positions on the Earth's surface today relies overwhelmingly on the measurement of time. Modern coordinate acquisition is predominantly achieved via the Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS), an umbrella term that includes the United States NAVSTAR Global Positioning System (GPS), the Russian GLONASS, the European Union's Galileo, and the Chinese BeiDou constellations.35

GNSS operates on the fundamental mathematical principle of trilateration.37 A receiver on the ground (the User Segment) calculates its absolute geometric position by measuring the precise arrival time of radio signals transmitted from multiple satellites orbiting in the Space Segment.35 Because these timing signals travel as electromagnetic waves at the constant speed of light (![][image71]), the spatial distance (![][image72]) between the satellite and the receiver is directly proportional to the time of flight (![][image73]), defined simply by the equation ![][image74].37

Consequently, spatial precision is inextricably, mercilessly linked to chronological precision. The speed of light is approximately ![][image75] kilometers per second. Therefore, a clock timing error of a mere one-thousandth of a second (![][image76]) translates to an enormous positioning error of 300 kilometers.37 A microscopic error of just 10 nanoseconds (![][image77]) introduces a spatial offset of 3 meters.39 To calculate a precise 3D position (latitude, longitude, elevation) and simultaneously solve for the receiver's own clock error, basic algebraic principles dictate that signals from a minimum of four satellites must be acquired simultaneously.37

### **Relativistic Adjustments in GNSS**

GNSS atomic clocks experience both motional and gravitational frequency shifts that are so massive that, without carefully accounting for numerous relativistic effects, the entire navigation system would fail within hours.35 Because the satellites are moving rapidly relative to the Earth's surface and exist in a much weaker gravitational field at high altitudes, their local proper time (![][image84]) diverges significantly from the coordinate time (![][image73]) established by reference clocks resting on the Earth's surface.35

The relativistic adjustments are governed primarily by two competing physical phenomena 35:

1. **Special Relativity (Time Dilation via Velocity):** GNSS satellites orbit the Earth at very high velocities (approximately 4 kilometers per second). According to the principles of special relativity, moving clocks tick slower relative to a stationary observer. This motional frequency shift, or second-order Doppler shift, is mathematically described by the factor ![][image85]. For a GPS satellite, this kinetic time dilation causes the satellite clock to lose approximately ![][image86] microseconds (![][image87]) per day relative to Earth.35  
2. **General Relativity (Gravitational Blueshift):** The satellites operate at high altitudes (roughly 20,184 km), where the Earth's gravitational potential (![][image18]) is significantly weaker than at the surface.40 According to the equivalence principle of general relativity, clocks situated in a weaker gravitational field tick faster. ### **Relativistic Geodesy and Chronometric Leveling**

The extreme sensitivity of the passage of time to gravitational potential has birthed an entirely new, highly experimental sub-discipline: relativistic geodesy.43 Historically, finding the precise difference in geopotential (i.e., true orthometric elevation) between two distant points required laborious classical optical leveling across the terrain, or complex regional gravimetric surveys utilizing the Stokes integral.14 However, the development of next-generation optical atomic clocks has opened the door to a revolutionary concept known as "chronometric leveling".43

Modern experimental optical atomic clocks, which utilize trapped ions (such as Strontium or ![][image102]) or neutral atoms suspended in optical lattices, provide extremely precise frequencies based on quantum energy transitions.44 These quantum sensors achieve mind-blowing fractional frequency uncertainties at the level of ![][image103] and are pushing toward ![][image104].44 At this extreme, unprecedented level of precision, comparing the tick rates of two optical clocks connected via a phase-stabilized optical fiber link directly reveals the gravitational redshift caused by the varying geopotential between their locations.44

Because general relativity dictates that this gravitational frequency shift is directly proportional to the geopotential difference, geodesists can calculate the exact difference in physical height between the two clock stations purely by measuring time.45 A fractional frequency stability of ![][image103] is roughly equivalent to measuring a geopotential difference equal to a 1-centimeter change in elevation.46 Recent groundbreaking field experiments have validated this. ## **Digital Elevation Models and the Modernization of Vertical Datums**

The monumental, interconnected efforts to map the Earth's geometry, measure its gravity anomalies, define the geoid through Stokes integration, and perfectly synchronize coordinates via relativistic GNSS timekeeping all culminate in the creation of highly accurate Digital Elevation Models (DEMs). DEMs are the critical, practical geospatial framework utilized for civil engineering, hydrological modeling, aerospace navigation, and environmental risk assessment.50

### **The Implications of Geoid Errors in DEMs**

A DEM is fundamentally a high-resolution spatial grid of elevation values. However, if those values are derived purely from geometric GNSS ellipsoidal heights without an accurately modeled geoid applied, the model fundamentally fails to represent physical reality.11 As established, water flows strictly according to geopotential gradients.10 A DEM lacking an accurate orthometric vertical datum will generate hydrological flow-path directions where waterways appear obstructed or where water attempts to flow geometrically "uphill" against gravity.10

This geodetic vulnerability is most pronounced and dangerous in dynamic coastal environments, where microscopic variations in true elevation dictate storm surge inundation patterns, saltwater intrusion, and the persistence of vital wetlands under conditions of global sea-level rise (SLR).53 In coastal modeling, the amount of water level increase projected for SLR is entirely analogous to a contour interval; a vertical error in the underlying DEM can completely skew the inundation assessment.55

A recent, comprehensive analysis of 385 peer-reviewed sea-level rise impact assessments revealed a staggering flaw: more than 90% of the assessments relied on assumed sea levels based on localized, unintegrated bare geoid models that drastically underestimated the coastal hazard.57 When high-resolution topographic terrain models (such as those derived from airborne LiDAR with a non-vegetated vertical root mean square error of 6.9 cm) are not meticulously and correctly integrated with highly accurate geoid and tidal models, the resulting conversion errors trigger immense cascading inaccuracies in static flood risk predictions.54 Image segmentation methods, such as region growing (or flood-fill algorithms) applied to DEMs to generate flood risk maps, will grossly overestimate or underestimate the inundation boundary if the baseline geopotential is misaligned.58

To eliminate these profound errors, the geodetic community demands that high-resolution global DEMs (such as those generated by the COPERNICUS, ALOS, or SRTM missions) continuously refine their vertical accuracy by referencing heavily refined, high-degree global geoid models like EGM96 or EGM2008.13 Integrating GNSS positioning with advanced satellite gravimetry ensures that the "zero" elevation baseline accurately reflects the complex, undulating physics of the Earth's actual oceans.13

### **Retiring NAVD 88 and the Transition to NAPGD2022**

To fully support modern, highly accurate GNSS spatial data and eliminate legacy vertical errors, national and international geodetic agencies are currently undertaking a massive overhaul of the world's vertical reference frames. In the United States, the legacy vertical datum relied upon by surveyors for decades is the North American Vertical Datum of 1988 (NAVD 88).12

NAVD 88 was physically realized through the direct occupation of hundreds of thousands of passive physical surveying benchmarks—brass disks pounded into the ground or set in concrete across the continent.12 However, this analog system suffers from severe, uncorrectable systematic errors. Over decades, the physical benchmarks have been subjected to natural crustal uplift, regional subsidence, and physical destruction.62 Furthermore, due to the accumulation of surveying errors during its creation, the entire NAVD 88 network contains a false, cross-country tilt of approximately half a meter from coast to coast.62

To rectify this unsustainable situation, the U.S. National Geodetic Survey (NGS) is undertaking a paradigm shift: replacing NAVD 88 entirely with the North American-Pacific Geopotential Datum of 2022 (NAPGD2022).60 The fundamental shift of NAPGD2022 is the total abandonment of reliance on passive physical marks in favor of a purely gravity-based, dynamic datum accessed primarily through active GNSS infrastructure (such as Continuously Operating Reference Stations, or CORS).61

NAPGD2022 is underpinned by the GRAV-D project (Gravity for the Redefinition of the American Vertical Datum), a massive, multi-year scientific initiative to collect high-resolution airborne gravity data over the entire United States and its territories.60 This exhaustive airborne gravity dataset has been mathematically combined with GRACE and GOCE satellite gravimetry and terrestrial absolute gravity checks to generate GEOID2022, a static geoid model expected to achieve an absolute vertical accuracy of 1-centimeter.60

With the implementation of NAPGD2022, a surveyor or GIS professional can determine a highly accurate orthometric elevation entirely from space without ever needing to locate a brass benchmark. The GNSS receiver computes the geometric ellipsoidal coordinate (![][image10]) using relativistic timekeeping, and the digital GEOID2022 model provides the precise gravitational offset (![][image12]), allowing for the calculation of true physical elevation (![][image11]) in mere seconds.62 Because this transition eliminates the legacy false tilt of NAVD 88, orthometric heights across the North American continent are expected to shift, in some areas by up to several decimeters, enforcing rigorous consistency across all modern geospatial datasets.60 Furthermore, GEOID2022 will be inherently time-dependent, mathematically accounting for the continuous temporal changes in gravity and regional subsidence.63

### **The International Height Reference System (IHRS)**

While the U.S. modernizes with NAPGD2022, vertical datums on a global scale have historically been intensely fractured. Traditionally, each nation established its own zero-elevation point based on localized tide gauges monitoring regional mean sea level.8 Because the physical surface of the ocean is not a perfect equipotential surface due to dynamic ocean topography, these national datums are not consistent in definition or level of accuracy.45 This results in frustrating geodetic discontinuities; for example, discrepancies exist between the vertical networks of the United States and Canada, and bridging oceanic areas to link individual height systems has been theoretically impossible.8

To resolve this global fragmentation, the International Association of Geodesy (IAG) released Resolution No. 1 during the 2015 General Assembly, officially defining the International Height Reference System (IHRS).65 The IHRS establishes a singular, conventional, gravity-field-related global height system to serve as an international standard for the precise determination of physical coordinates worldwide.65

The IHRS is a geopotential reference system co-rotating with the Earth. Instead of relying on localized sea level measurements, vertical coordinates in the IHRS are given directly by geopotential numbers (![][image106]). Crucially, the system anchors its "zero" reference to a specific, mathematically defined equipotential surface realized by the exact conventional value of ![][image107].65 By completely decoupling the vertical datum from localized mean sea level surrogates and tying it purely to the absolute physics of the geopotential and the spatial reference of the International Terrestrial Reference Frame (ITRF), the IHRS provides a seamless, unified framework for global science.8 This system enables the precise, mathematically rigorous comparison of global sea-level rise, tectonic crustal deformation, and global hydrology without the interference of localized datum discrepancies, uniting the geometric and physical pillars of geodesy into a single, cohesive global standard.
