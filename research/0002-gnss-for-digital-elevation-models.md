# **Advanced Global Navigation Satellite Systems in Digital Elevation Modeling: Methodologies, Sensor Integration, and Emerging Technologies**

The measurement, generation, and validation of Digital Elevation Models (DEMs) form the foundational geometric infrastructure for a vast array of geospatial, hydrological, geotechnical, and environmental applications. Over the past three decades, Global Navigation Satellite Systems (GNSS) have evolved from providing rudimentary navigational waypoints to serving as the absolute geometric backbone for high-precision topographic mapping. This profound evolution is driven by the modernization of satellite constellations, the proliferation of multi-frequency and multi-constellation receivers, the advent of advanced kinematic processing algorithms, and the integration of artificial intelligence in spatial data analysis. Furthermore, the rapid emergence of GNSS-Reflectometry (GNSS-R) has transformed navigation signals into opportunistic remote sensing tools, enabling direct altimetric measurements of the Earth's surface from space and airborne platforms. The following comprehensive analysis examines the theoretical geodetic principles, methodological frameworks, complex sensor integration strategies, and the comparative advantages of GNSS technologies in the contemporary context of DEM generation and environmental monitoring.

## **Geodetic Foundations: Height Systems, Reference Frames, and the Geoid**

The fundamental and most pervasive challenge in utilizing GNSS for absolute elevation mapping lies in the inherent discrepancy between the mathematical models utilized by satellite systems and the physical reality of the Earth's highly irregular gravity field. Resolving this geodetic discrepancy is the absolute prerequisite for generating hydrologically correct Digital Elevation Models, as errors at this foundational level will propagate systematically through all subsequent topographic datasets.

### **The Geometric Versus Physical Height Dichotomy**

GNSS positioning determines three-dimensional coordinates relative to a reference ellipsoid, which is a simplified, mathematically perfect oblate spheroid that approximates the overall shape of the Earth.1 The vertical distance between the phase center of a GNSS antenna and the surface of this reference ellipsoid—such as the World Geodetic System 1984 (WGS84) or the Geodetic Reference System 1980 (GRS80)—is known as the ellipsoidal height or geodetic height.1 While ellipsoidal models provide a highly efficient, unified global computational framework, they are purely geometric abstractions that completely ignore the Earth's heterogeneous internal mass distribution and the resulting fluctuations in its gravity field.1 Consequently, ellipsoidal heights are purely spatial and do not dictate the direction of fluid flow; based solely on ellipsoidal heights, water could theoretically flow "uphill".2

Conversely, practical engineering, floodplain inundation modeling, and topographically accurate DEMs require physical height systems, which are inextricably linked to the Earth's geopotential field.5 The most critical reference surface in this physical domain is the geoid. The geoid is defined as an equipotential surface of the Earth's gravity field that best approximates global mean sea level (MSL) in the absence of dynamically disturbing forces such as tides, winds, and ocean currents, extending theoretically beneath the continental landmasses.1 The vertical distance measured along the curved plumb line from a specific point on the Earth's surface down to the geoid is defined as the orthometric height, which represents the practical elevation values required by surveyors and engineers.1

## **The GNSS Error Budget and Atmospheric Signal Distortions**

While the theoretical 3D geometry of GNSS positioning appears computationally straightforward, the physical propagation of radio frequency (RF) signals from satellite orbits approximately 20,200 km above the Earth introduces a complex matrix of signal delays and atmospheric distortions.16 In the context of elevation mapping, vertical accuracy is historically recognized to be 1.5 to 3 times poorer than horizontal accuracy. This degradation occurs due to satellite geometry—receivers can only track satellites located above the horizon, preventing the optimal 360-degree spherical geometric intersection required for perfect 3D trilateration—and because the vertical component is significantly more sensitive to atmospheric delays.18

The most significant source of signal degradation stems from the Earth's atmosphere. The ionosphere acts as a dispersive medium for radio frequency signals; therefore, the delay introduced is entirely frequency-dependent.20 The troposphere, containing the bulk of the planet's weather systems and water vapor, is a non-dispersive medium that delays all frequencies equally, heavily dependent on the satellite's elevation angle relative to the horizon.19

### **Multipath Vulnerabilities in Topographic Mapping**

For high-resolution topographic mapping in complex environments, multipath propagation remains one of the most formidable vulnerabilities.22 Multipath occurs when the GNSS signal reflects off local terrain features, dense forest canopy, or building facades before reaching the receiving antenna.22 This reflection creates a condition where the receiver tracks a delayed, out-of-phase echo alongside the direct Line-Of-Sight (LOS) signal.23

In basic code-phase measurements, severe multipath can introduce vertical errors ranging from a few meters to tens of meters.22 In highly precise carrier-phase measurements—which are an absolute requirement for centimeter-level DEM generation—multipath errors are geometrically smaller (typically around 1 centimeter for L1 frequencies) but can trigger a complete loss of integer ambiguity resolution.23 When a receiver loses its "FIX" solution due to canopy occlusion or multipath distortion, the vertical accuracy is immediately severely degraded, introducing massive spikes and unreliable elevation data into the survey.24 Consequently, in densely vegetated areas, signal occlusion combined with multipath effectively limits the utility of ground-based GNSS surveying, forcing operators to rely on airborne active sensors.25

### **Multi-Constellation and Multi-Frequency Mitigation Strategies**

The primary technological strategy for neutralizing these errors in modern topographic surveying is the utilization of multi-constellation, multi-frequency (MCMF) receivers. The deployment of complementary global networks—including the U.S. GPS, Russian GLONASS, European Galileo, and Chinese BeiDou—has vastly expanded the number of visible satellites from roughly 8 to over 40 at any given location.27 This vast increase significantly improves the Geometric Dilution of Precision (GDOP), ensuring robust satellite intersection geometries even when portions of the sky are occluded by deep valleys or urban canyons.29

Furthermore, modern geodetic receivers leverage multiple signal frequencies broadcast by these satellites.29 Because ionospheric delay is dispersive and frequency-dependent, receivers can mathematically form an "ionosphere-free linear combination" by comparing the precise arrival times of two distinct frequencies.29 This algorithmic technique effectively cancels out first-order ionospheric errors without relying on external modeling.

The introduction of modernized wideband signals, particularly the GPS L5 and Galileo E5 bands, represents a critical leap forward for high-resolution DEM surveys.33 These signals possess superior correlation properties, higher chipping rates, and substantially wider bandwidths (up to 20 MHz), rendering them inherently more resilient to multipath interference and deliberate signal jamming compared to legacy L1 coarse/acquisition (C/A) codes.23 Research assessing blanking duty cycles and multipath suppression confirms that Galileo's E5 AltBOC signals provide unparalleled robustness for kinematic tracking in challenging environments.33

## **Advanced Kinematic Positioning Methodologies**

To achieve the stringent centimeter-level vertical accuracy required for high-fidelity DEMs, the standalone positioning paradigms described above are inadequate. Surveyors and photogrammetrists must employ differential carrier-phase tracking techniques that computationally resolve the integer ambiguity of the radio waveforms. Three primary methodologies dominate modern topographic mapping operations: Real-Time Kinematic (RTK), Post-Processed Kinematic (PPK), and Precise Point Positioning (PPP).36

### **Real-Time Kinematic (RTK) Infrastructure**

RTK utilizes a static base station positioned precisely over a known geodetic control point. This base station calculates localized atmospheric, orbital, and clock errors in real time by comparing its known position to the GNSS observables, subsequently transmitting these correction parameters via a continuous radio link or cellular network (NTRIP) to a roving receiver.36 By processing the carrier-phase observables dynamically, RTK achieves instantaneous 3D positional accuracies within 1 to 3 centimeters.18

However, RTK possesses distinct operational limitations for large-scale DEM mapping. It absolutely requires an unbroken, high-bandwidth communication link between the base and the rover.18 In rugged topography, deep river valleys, or dense forest ecosystems, line-of-sight radio links frequently fail, and commercial cellular coverage is typically non-existent. When this critical telemetry link drops, the system defaults back to an uncorrected standalone solution, instantly ruining the spatial elevation profile of the resulting DEM.18

### **Post-Processed Kinematic (PPK) Resilience**

PPK mitigates the telemetry vulnerabilities inherent to RTK by eliminating the requirement for a real-time data link entirely. Under the PPK paradigm, both the static base station and the kinematic roving unit independently log raw GNSS satellite observables internally throughout the duration of the survey mission.18 Following the flight or ground survey, these massive data streams are temporally aligned and merged within specialized post-processing software.38

PPK is inherently more robust for complex topographic mapping. By processing the data iteratively forward and backward in time, PPK algorithms can algorithmically interpolate through minor signal blockages, maintain carrier-phase lock over much longer baselines (sometimes up to 100 km), and yield statistically superior and more consistent vertical accuracies than RTK.18 Consequently, for UAV-based LiDAR and photogrammetry mapping over difficult terrain—especially where Beyond Visual Line of Sight (BVLOS) operations are conducted—PPK has become the gold standard for securing the geometric integrity of the mapping block without the risk of signal dropout.36

### **Precise Point Positioning (PPP) and High Accuracy Services**

PPP diverges sharply from both RTK and PPK by eliminating the need for a local physical base station entirely.37 Instead, PPP relies on a vast global network of reference stations to generate highly precise, real-time satellite clock and orbital trajectory corrections, which are delivered to the user via satellite L-band transmission or over the internet.37 Historically, PPP algorithms required convergence times of up to 30 to 45 minutes to mathematically resolve atmospheric biases before achieving centimeter-level accuracy, limiting its utility for rapid, highly dynamic kinematic mapping.42

This paradigm is currently undergoing a radical transformation with the advent of advanced State Space Representation (SSR) architectures and the implementation of the Galileo High Accuracy Service (HAS).41 Declared initially operational in early 2023, Galileo HAS Phase 1 provides free, globally accessible PPP corrections broadcast directly via the Galileo E6-B signal and over the internet.43 Utilizing these SSR corrections, multi-constellation receivers can achieve horizontal accuracies of ![][image15] cm and vertical accuracies of ![][image16] cm without any reliance on local base stations, with convergence times dropping below 300 seconds.42

The impending HAS Phase 2, slated for full operational capability in 2026, promises to deliver regional atmospheric ionospheric corrections directly to the rover.42 This upgrade is engineered to reduce convergence times from 300 seconds down to under 100 seconds.42 This revolutionary advancement is poised to democratize access to high-precision DEM data, particularly in remote regions, deep oceans, or developing nations lacking the capital to deploy dense Continuously Operating Reference Station (CORS) networks.47 Concurrent with this infrastructure development, the market is experiencing a democratization of hardware, with the proliferation of low-cost, dual-frequency GNSS chips (such as the u-blox ZED-F9P series) integrated into standard drones and mobile mapping devices, bringing survey-grade precision to a broader range of scientific and commercial users.49

## **Sensor Fusion: Integrating Kinematic GNSS with Airborne LiDAR and Photogrammetry**

While ground-based GNSS receivers mounted on survey poles provide ultimate point-level precision, they are drastically constrained by their spatial sampling density; walking a grid over thousands of hectares is economically and logistically unfeasible.26 To generate high-resolution, continuous DEMs, the geospatial industry relies on the tight integration of kinematic GNSS with active remote sensing payloads mounted on airborne platforms—primarily Airborne Laser Scanning (LiDAR) and high-resolution aerial photogrammetry.53

### **Direct Georeferencing and Trajectory Estimation**

Historically, airborne mapping relied on indirect georeferencing, requiring dense networks of physically surveyed Ground Control Points (GCPs) to anchor aerial images via a complex bundle block adjustment.39 Modern airborne topographic mapping, however, relies entirely on Direct Georeferencing (DG), where the external orientation parameters—the precise X, Y, Z coordinates and the Roll, Pitch, Yaw attitudes—of the sensor are determined dynamically in the air.55

This direct determination is achieved through a tightly coupled integration of a kinematic GNSS receiver and an Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU).54 The GNSS unit provides absolute, low-frequency positional drift correction, establishing the global coordinate frame.55 Concurrently, the IMU—consisting of high-frequency gyroscopes and accelerometers operating at hundreds of hertz—tracks the rapid micro-movements, vibrations, and rotational dynamics of the aircraft or UAV between the slower GNSS epoch updates.57 This synergistic data fusion, typically governed by an Unscented or Extended Kalman Filter within the Position and Orientation System (POS), yields a highly refined, unbroken 3D trajectory.57

### **Boresight Misalignment and Lever-Arm Calibration**

The geometric accuracy of a resulting LiDAR point cloud or photogrammetric DEM is exceptionally sensitive to the precise spatial relationship between the GNSS antenna, the IMU origin, and the sensor's optical center.56

1. **Lever-Arm Vectors:** The exact three-dimensional spatial offsets (![][image17]) between the phase center of the GNSS antenna and the center of navigation of the IMU must be rigidly measured to millimeter precision.61 Any error in measuring the lever-arm vector acts as a physical lever, exponentially amplifying positional error on the ground as the aircraft pitches and rolls during flight.  
2. **Boresight Calibration:** Boresight misalignment refers to the microscopic angular differences in the coordinate frames between the IMU and the optical axis of the LiDAR scanner or photogrammetric camera.56 Because a laser pulse must travel hundreds of meters to the ground, an angular boresight error of just ![][image18] at a flying altitude of 1000 meters translates to a planar shift of nearly 20 centimeters on the Earth's surface.60

To mitigate these systemic errors, modern automated Aerial Triangulation (AT) software incorporates rigorous self-calibration routines.54 The process involves flying specific intersecting flight line patterns over a highly controlled calibration site featuring known 3D geometry.57 The processing software iteratively adjusts the bundle block, solving simultaneously for the lever-arm parameters, boresight angles, time-synchronization delays, and tie-point coordinates.64 Using an initial, coarse DEM derived from the first pass helps refine the ray-tracing projection during this AT process, dramatically improving vertical accuracy in areas of complex, rugged relief.54

### **Surface Interpolation: Kriging vs. Inverse Distance Weighting**

Once the dense kinematic point cloud is generated and filtered to isolate bare-earth ground returns, the discrete data points must be mathematically interpolated to create a continuous raster DEM grid.67 For sparser GNSS field data or heavily filtered LiDAR datasets in dense forests, the choice of interpolation algorithm heavily dictates the morphological fidelity of the resulting topographic surface.

Empirical studies comparing standard interpolation methods—such as Kriging, Inverse Distance Weighting (IDW), and Local Polynomial Interpolation (LPI)—reveal distinct behavioral traits based on terrain type. IDW operates under a strict deterministic assumption that values closer to a sampled point are more related than those further away, applying a linear mathematical decay.67 While computationally efficient, IDW performs poorly in areas of steep topographic relief, frequently introducing artificial "bullseye" artifacts around sampled points and struggling to accurately infer peaks or valleys that fall between survey lines.69

Kriging, conversely, is a highly sophisticated geostatistical method that incorporates spatial autocorrelation.69 By utilizing a semivariogram to actively quantify the spatial variance of the elevation data as a function of distance and direction, Ordinary Kriging assigns optimal, statistically sound weights to surrounding points.70 Empirical field assessments indicate that Kriging significantly outperforms IDW in modeling complex terrain; in one comparative study, Kriging reduced total Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) margins in steep slope areas to roughly 26 cm, while LPI and Kriging methods achieved sub-10 cm levels in flat terrain, demonstrating Kriging's ability to adapt to shape configurations that respect regional geomorphology.67

## **GNSS-Reflectometry (GNSS-R): Transcending Navigation into Active Remote Sensing**

For decades, traditional GNSS positioning methods treated signal reflections from the ground as an insidious error source to be aggressively filtered out. However, the emerging scientific field of GNSS-Reflectometry (GNSS-R) actively exploits these reflections as a potent form of bistatic radar.72 By analyzing L-band signals that have bounced off the Earth's surface before reaching a receiver located on the ground, mounted on an aircraft, or orbiting in low-Earth orbit (LEO), GNSS-R can extract vital biogeophysical parameters. This includes precise sea surface heights, river slope gradients, soil moisture profiles, and ice sheet elevations.72

### **SNR Methods and Signal Polarization**

Advanced ground-based methodologies, such as GNSS Interferometric Reflectometry (GNSS-IR), utilize a single Right-Handed Circular Polarization (RHCP) antenna to measure the interference pattern created by the continuous combination of direct and reflected signals.77 As a satellite rises or sets, the changing elevation angle produces cyclic, wave-like modulations in the receiver's Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) data stream.72 The dominant frequency of this interference pattern is directly proportional to the vertical distance between the antenna's phase center and the reflecting surface, allowing for continuous, sub-centimeter monitoring of tidal variations, sea level anomalies, or snow depth without the need for complex dual-antenna setups.72

When L-band signals reflect off rough surfaces at high elevation angles, the electromagnetic polarization typically shifts from Right-Handed to Left-Handed Circular Polarization (LHCP).77 Conventional dual-antenna GNSS-R payloads exploit this physical phenomenon by utilizing a dedicated LHCP antenna pointing nadir to cleanly isolate the reflected component from the direct RHCP signal.77

### **Delay-Doppler Maps (DDMs) and Deep Learning Inversion**

Spaceborne GNSS-R missions map the reflected energy across a two-dimensional grid of time delays and Doppler frequency shifts, creating a product known as a Delay-Doppler Map (DDM).84 The morphological spread of energy across the DDM dictates the physical characteristics of the surface below. A calm ocean, smooth lake, or flat ice sheet generates a sharp, coherent peak representing specular reflection, which allows for precise altimetric phase tracking and high spatial resolution.81 Conversely, a rough ocean surface agitated by wind, or a dense forest canopy, results in incoherent, diffuse scattering. This spreads the reflected power across multiple delay and Doppler bins, generating a characteristic "horseshoe" shape in the DDM that scientists use to derive ocean wind speeds or estimate above-ground biomass.87

Missions such as NASA's Cyclone GNSS (CYGNSS), the UK's TechDemoSat-1 (TDS-1), the recent Triton mission (launched October 2023), and emerging constellations from commercial providers like Spire have proven the immense scientific viability of spaceborne GNSS-R.72 Because these satellites act purely as passive receivers of opportunity, utilizing signals already broadcast by GPS and Galileo, they require minimal onboard power and mass. This makes them highly suitable for deployment on low-cost CubeSat constellations.74 This constellation configuration achieves temporal revisit rates of just a few hours, a monumental advantage over traditional, massive radar satellites.92

To process the massive influx of complex DDM data, researchers are increasingly deploying advanced artificial intelligence and deep learning architectures. Transformer-based fusion networks (TF-NET), U-Net configurations, and Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) are now utilized to predict DDM patterns in unobserved regions, segment glacial lakes from optical and GNSS-R data, and drastically improve the precision of soil moisture retrievals by discovering non-linear correlations in the scattered signal data.94 Future missions, such as the European Space Agency's HydroGNSS (slated for launch in 2025), are expressly dedicated to mapping terrestrial hydrological variables, utilizing these advanced algorithms to enhance global DEMs with continuous data on soil moisture, wetlands, and permafrost dynamics.72

## **Comparative Analysis: GNSS, LiDAR, and InSAR in Elevation Mapping**

The generation of highly accurate regional or global DEMs relies upon a triad of remote sensing modalities: GNSS, Light Detection and Ranging (LiDAR), and Interferometric Synthetic Aperture Radar (InSAR). Each technology possesses highly specific strengths, spatial profiles, and fundamental physical limitations, dictating their optimal deployment scenarios.98

1. **Airborne LiDAR:** LiDAR operates as an active monostatic sensor, emitting rapid laser pulses in the near-infrared spectrum.101 It delivers unparalleled absolute spatial resolution and structural detail, generating point densities frequently exceeding 50 points per square meter when flown from low-altitude UAVs.55 LiDAR excels in forestry and vegetation management due to its ability to penetrate canopy gaps to map the bare earth beneath, yielding typical vertical accuracies of 10 to 15 centimeters.100 However, LiDAR is limited by cloud cover, cannot penetrate rain or fog, and relies completely on the onboard kinematic GNSS/IMU integration for its absolute geometric accuracy.104  
2. **InSAR:** InSAR relies on microwave radar signals, comparing the interferometric phase differences between two satellite passes over the exact same terrain to measure sub-millimeter surface displacements over time.99 While InSAR is unmatched for monitoring dynamic subsidence, glacial flow, or tectonic deformation, its utility for generating absolute static DEMs is limited.106 SAR-derived DEMs (such as the SRTM, ALOS PALSAR, or TanDEM-X products) generally suffer from lower spatial resolution (ranging from 12m to 30m grid spacing) and exhibit significant vertical artifacts in mountainous terrain due to radar layover and shadowing effects.105 In a comparative study, the standard deviation of an InSAR DEM reached 2.604 meters in complex terrain, reflecting higher vertical inaccuracy compared to photogrammetric or LiDAR alternatives.110 Furthermore, short-wavelength radar (X-band, C-band) reflects off the top of forest canopies rather than the bare earth, leading to gross overestimations of elevation in densely vegetated zones.105  
3. **GNSS-R:** Spaceborne GNSS-R bridges a unique observational gap. While its static spatial resolution over land is currently much lower than LiDAR (often resolving specular footprints of roughly 1 km to 7 km, though ocean resolution is closer to 25 km) 81, its vast swath width, weather-independence, cost-effectiveness, and rapid revisit times make it highly potent.92 It is explicitly designed for tracking highly dynamic surface changes, such as floodplain inundation or massive ice sheet mass balances, where temporal frequency heavily outweighs the need for sub-meter planar resolution.92

### **Sensor Fusion and ASPRS Accuracy Standards**

Modern mapping methodologies actively fuse these diverse datasets to overcome individual sensor limitations. Studies consistently demonstrate that fusing high-resolution airborne LiDAR (to establish foundational structural detail) with satellite InSAR (for ongoing temporal deformation analysis), and anchoring the entire dataset with absolute GNSS PPP time-series arrays, produces the ultimate 4D geohazard mapping product.99

To maintain scientific integrity, the American Society for Photogrammetry and Remote Sensing (ASPRS) defines stringent guidelines for validating these hybrid datasets. According to the ASPRS Positional Accuracy Standards for Digital Geospatial Data (Edition 2, 2023), ground control points (GCPs) established via GNSS must possess a verified accuracy that is at least twice as tight as the target accuracy of the final LiDAR or photogrammetry DEM.116 Furthermore, robust validation mandates a minimum of 30 independent, evenly distributed survey checkpoints across the project area.116 The standards strictly require separating Non-Vegetated Vertical Accuracy (NVA) from Vegetated Vertical Accuracy (VVA), ensuring that systemic canopy bias is statistically isolated and evaluated independently through individual elevation differences rather than a combined RMSE value.116

## **Applied Topographic Case Studies: Cryosphere, Hydrology, and Coastal Dynamics**

The theoretical integration of GNSS geometries, differential correction pipelines, and multipath reflectometry culminates in the deployment of these systems to monitor some of the Earth's most volatile, critical, and inaccessible environments.

### **Cryosphere and Glacier Mass Balance Dynamics**

Glaciers present highly unique surveying challenges due to extreme topography, high surface albedo, lack of ground control access, and rapid kinetic deformation. Precise monitoring of ice volume loss is absolutely critical for modeling local watershed supplies and forecasting global sea-level rise.119 Traditional methods relying on sporadic optical satellite imagery lack the resolution required for detailed mass-balance studies and are frequently hindered by cloud cover.

Recent field deployments utilize PPK-enabled UAVs flying directly over remote ice fields, such as the debris-covered Belvedere Glacier in the Italian Alps and proglacial lake systems in the Peruvian Andes.119 By bypassing the need for hazardous manual GCP placement on moving ice, PPK UAVs capture sub-decimeter accuracy orthomosaics and DEMs.119 Through multi-temporal differencing of these high-resolution DEMs, geomorphologists can extract highly granular vector fields detailing ice flow velocities (ranging significantly from 2 to 22 meters per year) and volumetric subsidence metrics (registering losses of 2 to 3.5 million cubic meters of ice annually at the Belvedere site).119

Furthermore, automated "smart stakes" equipped with GNSS receivers, ultrasonic sensors, and Iridium satellite telemetry are now deployed directly onto glaciers, such as the Place Glacier in British Columbia.122 These low-cost, in-situ GNSS devices deliver high-temporal resolution profiles of surface ablation during extreme heat events, validating and scaling the spatial models derived from periodic airborne LiDAR flights.122 Concurrently, spaceborne GNSS-R platforms have successfully generated large-scale DEMs of the vast Antarctic and Greenland ice sheets. Utilizing grazing angle reflections, GNSS-R has mapped the South Pole with root-mean-square errors under 33 meters compared to NASA's Operation IceBridge LiDAR datasets.112 The highly specular nature of flat ice reflection permits highly coherent GNSS-R signal returns, enabling polarimetric extraction of sea-ice thickness, extent, and type.81

### **Coastal Altimetry and Hydro-Hazards**

The global coastal zone is highly vulnerable to eustatic sea-level rise, destructive tsunamis, and intense storm surges.78 Conventional nadir-looking satellite radar altimeters (like the Jason series) frequently struggle to provide accurate measurements within 5 to 10 kilometers of the coastline due to inhomogeneous microwave reflectance caused by land interference creeping into the sensor's footprint.77

GNSS Interferometric Reflectometry (GNSS-IR) provides a revolutionary geodetic tool for bridging this coastal measurement gap. By retrofitting existing coastal GNSS CORS (Continuously Operating Reference Stations) infrastructure to actively monitor the interference patterns of reflected signals, researchers effectively transform standard geodetic ground stations into dense, highly accurate tide-gauge networks.80 GNSS-IR operates independently of land subsidence—a major confounding variable that plagues mechanical stilling-well tide gauges—because GNSS intrinsically ties the sea surface elevation to an absolute, global geocentric reference frame.126 Experimental installations, such as those at the Onsala Space Observatory, demonstrate that GNSS-based tide gauges achieve a root-mean-square agreement on the order of just 4 centimeters compared to traditional gauges located tens of kilometers away.126 These networks have successfully tracked high-frequency transient events, validating sub-centimeter level agreements with traditional gauges during the severe storm surges of Hurricane Harvey and detecting rapid tsunami wavefronts induced by seismic events.128

Similarly, spaceborne GNSS-R data is increasingly assimilated into numerical weather models to map inland flooding and trace river slope dynamics over vast, inaccessible territories.75 The L-band signals of the GNSS constellation easily penetrate dense cloud cover and thick vegetation canopies to detect surface inundation changes rapidly.89 During catastrophic rainfall events, missions like CYGNSS provide highly valuable bistatic radar cross-sections that precisely delineate flood extents underneath agricultural and forest vegetation, providing critical terrain mapping intelligence to humanitarian and disaster response teams where optical sensors are entirely blinded by weather.80

## **Conclusion**

The science of measuring and generating digital elevation models has undergone a fundamental paradigm shift, driven entirely by the expanding capabilities and applications of Global Navigation Satellite Systems. GNSS is no longer relegated solely to establishing sparse, static ground control networks; it currently operates as the central nervous system of modern geomatics. The tightly coupled integration of kinematic GNSS, high-frequency Inertial Measurement Units, and airborne LiDAR allows for the direct georeferencing of dense point clouds with sub-decimeter fidelity. Simultaneously, continuous advancements in multi-constellation signal processing and state-space representation (SSR) architectures—most notably the rollout of Galileo's High Accuracy Service—are rapidly eroding the historic necessity for local physical base stations, promising a future of global, real-time Precise Point Positioning.

Furthermore, the rapid ascent of GNSS-Reflectometry illustrates a profound repurposing of the L-band electromagnetic spectrum. By actively weaponizing the physics of multipath interference, GNSS-R has established a highly scalable, exceptionally low-cost framework for dynamic spaceborne altimetry, proving uniquely capable of sensing critical climate variables across ocean, land, and cryosphere environments. As data streams from sophisticated interpolation algorithms, advanced satellite gravimetry, and autonomous aerial platforms continue to converge, the spatial resolution and absolute geometric accuracy of global topographic mapping will continuously refine, providing the exact empirical foundation required to manage the Earth's shifting geology and accelerating climate dynamics in the 21st century.
