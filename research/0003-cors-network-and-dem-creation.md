# **The Foundational Role of the Continuously Operating Reference Station (CORS) Network in Digital Elevation Model (DEM) Generation and Evaluation**

## **Introduction to Geodetic Infrastructure and Topographic Modeling**

The precise measurement of the Earth's surface—its shape, orientation, mass distribution, and topographic undulations—relies entirely on a robust, highly maintained underlying geodetic infrastructure. At the absolute forefront of this infrastructure within the United States and its territories is the Continuously Operating Reference Station (CORS) Network, formally managed by the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration's (NOAA) National Geodetic Survey (NGS) as the NOAA CORS Network (NCN).1 The NCN represents a multi-agency, multi-purpose cooperative endeavor that aggregates Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS) carrier phase and code range measurements from independently owned and operated stations.2 By providing a continuously updated, highly stable spatial reference framework, the CORS network allows geospatial practitioners to reduce GNSS positioning errors from the scale of meters down to centimeters or even millimeters.4

This extraordinary level of positional fidelity is the absolute prerequisite for the modern generation and evaluation of Digital Elevation Models (DEMs). A DEM is a digital cartographic representation of the elevation of the terrain—specifically the Z-dimension—at discrete intervals across a specified geographic area.5 DEMs are indispensable tools for a myriad of complex applications, including flood hazard modeling, civil engineering, infrastructure layout, agricultural planning, and nuanced earth science research such as monitoring rapidly changing snowpack depths.6 The generation of these high-fidelity models, whether through airborne Light Detection and Ranging (LiDAR), synthetic aperture radar (SAR), or Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) Structure-from-Motion (SfM) photogrammetry, inherently relies on kinematic GNSS trajectory data.5 Without the static, highly accurate base station data provided by the CORS network to post-process and differential-correct the airborne trajectories, the resulting topographic models would float untethered from the true geodetic framework of the Earth, rendering them virtually useless for high-precision engineering or temporal change detection.

Furthermore, as the geospatial community relies increasingly on global, open-source DEM datasets—such as the Shuttle Radar Topography Mission (SRTM), the Advanced Spaceborne Thermal Emission and Reflection Radiometer (ASTER) Global DEM, and the Advanced Land Observing Satellite World 3D (AW3D30)—the CORS network serves a vital secondary function. It acts as the ultimate, authoritative "ground truth" repository. By comparing the elevation values of these global DEMs against the highly precise, mathematically rigorous ellipsoid and orthometric heights of CORS monuments, researchers can perform absolute vertical accuracy assessments across diverse terrain and land cover classifications.9

This comprehensive report provides an exhaustive examination of the CORS network from the specialized perspective of DEM generation and evaluation. It explores the underlying architecture of the network, the rigorous workflows integrating CORS data with remote sensing platforms, the theoretical frameworks governing signal error propagation, and the evolving regulatory standards—such as the newly updated American Society for Photogrammetry and Remote Sensing (ASPRS) Positional Accuracy Standards—that dictate how DEM accuracy must be quantified.13 It further addresses the critical implications of tectonic plate motion and dynamic datums on elevation modeling, particularly in highly active seismic zones, and concludes with a robust discussion on the pedagogical strategies necessary to teach these complex, interrelated geodetic disciplines to the next generation of scientists, engineers, and geospatial professionals.14

## **The Architecture and Operational Mandate of the CORS Network**

The NOAA CORS Network (NCN) is not a singular, monolithic hardware array owned and maintained by a solitary entity. Rather, it functions as an integrated, cooperative web comprising over a thousand GNSS reference stations operated by hundreds of government, academic, and private organizations.2 Each participating agency voluntarily shares its dual-frequency GNSS data streams and highly specific station metadata with the NGS, which subsequently analyzes, archives, and distributes the data free of charge to the public domain.2 This monumental dataset is accessible via traditional NGS servers as well as modern cloud infrastructure, such as Amazon Web Services (AWS), containing continuous historical observations dating back to February 9, 1994\.3

The primary purpose of the NCN is to provide definitive, continuous support for the National Spatial Reference System (NSRS), defining latitude, longitude, and height accurately throughout the United States.18 To maintain the strict integrity required for high-precision surveying and photogrammetric mapping, the NGS imposes rigid performance goals on the network. Stations incorporated into the foundational tier of the network must provide definitional support for the International Terrestrial Reference Frame (ITRF) and global tectonic plate rotation models.19 They must be subjected to rigorous site surveys adhering strictly to International Earth Rotation and Reference Systems Service (IERS) standards on a mandated five-year cycle.19 Furthermore, operational uptime is heavily monitored and enforced; the overarching network aims for an availability of greater than 90% at all times, with individual station downtime strictly restricted to less than 14 consecutive days to prevent spatial data gaps.19

This continuous, highly scrutinized tracking is critical because the Earth is not a static body. The geodetic reference systems that historically underpinned mapping efforts in North America are currently undergoing a revolutionary modernization process, driven almost entirely by the data harvested from the CORS network.

## **Geodetic Datums and the Modernization of the NSRS**

The North American Datum of 1983 (NAD 83\) and the North American Vertical Datum of 1988 (NAVD 88), while serving as the official horizontal and vertical datums of the NSRS for decades, possess profound, mathematically quantifiable shortcomings that directly impact DEM accuracy.18 NAD 83 is non-geocentric, meaning it is physically misaligned to the true center of the Earth's mass by approximately 2.2 meters relative to modern global frames like ITRF2014.18 NAVD 88 is similarly flawed; because it was constrained to local mean sea level at a single tide gauge, it inherently fails to account for the complex variations between local mean sea level and the true global geopotential surface (the geoid).18 This legacy methodology resulted in a systemic elevation bias of roughly half a meter and an artificial continental tilt that approaches one full meter from the Atlantic to the Pacific coast.18

Recognizing these systemic errors, the NGS relies heavily on the CORS network to monitor crustal dynamics and facilitate the imminent modernization of the NSRS. The impending transition will replace NAD 83 with a suite of new plate-specific Terrestrial Reference Frames, and will replace NAVD 88 with a geopotential datum based strictly on high-resolution gravity models rather than physical leveling marks.21 Because these new systems are inherently tied to active GNSS observations and continuously refined geopotential models, the millimeter-level velocity tracking provided by CORS stations serves as the absolute bedrock upon which all future digital elevation modeling in the United States will be vertically referenced.18

| Legacy Reference System | Modernized 2022 Replacement | Primary Deficiencies Addressed |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **North American Datum of 1983 (NAD 83\)** | North American Terrestrial Reference Frame of 2022 (NATRF2022) | Resolves the \~2.2-meter non-geocentric offset; aligns perfectly with the Earth's true center of mass and modern GNSS satellite orbits.18 |
| **NAD 83 (Pacific, Mariana, Caribbean)** | PATRF2022, MATRF2022, CATRF2022 | Provides specific tectonic plate rotation models for island territories, preventing coordinate drift relative to the local landmass.21 |

To facilitate the conversion of legacy DEMs and historical LiDAR point clouds into these modernized frames, the NGS has developed advanced transformation tools, including the NGS Coordinate Conversion and Transformation Tool (NCAT).21 Integral to NCAT is VERTCON 3.0, which allows for the rigorous mathematical conversion of vertical data from legacy datums (such as the National Geodetic Vertical Datum of 1929 and NAVD 88\) to the current and future vertical datums.23 Furthermore, modern geoid models like GEOID18 (superseding GEOID12B in the contiguous United States) are utilized to accurately convert the ellipsoidal heights derived directly from CORS GNSS receivers into highly usable orthometric heights, forming the critical bridge between satellite measurements and physical terrain mapping.24

## **Integrating CORS with Remote Sensing for DEM Creation**

The creation of modern, high-resolution Digital Elevation Models relies almost exclusively on active and passive remote sensing technologies—predominantly airborne LiDAR and UAV-based photogrammetry.5 Regardless of the specific sensor modality utilized, the spatial accuracy of the resulting DEM is entirely, mathematically dependent on the precise positioning of the sensor platform in three-dimensional space at the exact microsecond of data acquisition.

### **The Kinematic Trajectory and PPK Workflows**

When an aircraft or a fixed-wing UAV flies over a project area—for instance, capturing imagery to tackle local flood studies or mapping rapidly changing snow depth in forested ecosystems—it continuously records GNSS and Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) data to build a navigation trajectory.5 However, the raw, autonomous GNSS data collected by the aircraft's onboard receiver is subject to standard atmospheric delays (ionospheric and tropospheric refraction), satellite clock errors, and orbital ephemeris errors, generally resulting in real-time positional uncertainties of several meters. To achieve the sub-decimeter accuracy mandated for high-quality DEM creation, this raw trajectory must be differentially corrected against a known, highly static point.26

Historically, this required deploying proprietary physical base stations within a 10-kilometer radius of the project area, a practice fraught with logistical complexities, equipment security risks, and flight scheduling delays.27 The expansion and densification of the CORS network have largely eliminated this requirement. Surveyors and photogrammetrists can now simply download Receiver Independent Exchange Format (RINEX) data from the nearest CORS stations that geographically bracket the project area and temporally overlap the flight mission.27

During the data post-processing phase, specialized trajectory software (such as Applanix POSPac) combines the airborne GNSS/IMU data with the high-rate, 1-second interval RINEX data downloaded from the CORS network.28 By resolving the integer ambiguities of the carrier phase signals between the stationary CORS base and the moving aerial rover, a highly precise, smoothed best estimate of trajectory (SBET) is generated through a mathematical process known as Post-Processed Kinematic (PPK).29 State Departments of Transportation, such as Caltrans, have successfully validated and incorporated archived CORS data into their aerial triangulation procedures to completely eliminate the need for physical field base stations, streamlining operations for routine corridor mapping and emergency response surveying.27

This precise SBET trajectory is subsequently utilized to geographically anchor the remote sensing data. For LiDAR applications, the trajectory is fused with the laser range and scan angle measurements to generate a massive, rigorously georeferenced point cloud, typically output as a Log ASCII Standard (LAS) file.29 For photogrammetric applications, the trajectory provides precise camera projection centers (the exact X, Y, and Z position of the camera at the moment of each exposure). This dramatically reduces the need for dense, ground-surveyed control points in the subsequent aerial triangulation bundle block adjustment, allowing software utilizing Structure-from-Motion (SfM) algorithms to generate highly accurate terrain representations.5 In novel hybrid approaches, practitioners utilize the highly accurate, CORS-corrected LiDAR data to generate LiDAR-derived control points (LCPs), which are then injected into UAV photogrammetry workflows to semi-automatically calibrate the optical imagery without requiring physical targets on the ground.5

### **Distinguishing DSMs from DEMs and the Criticality of Filtering**

It is paramount for practitioners to differentiate between the types of elevation models generated by these varying technologies. UAV photogrammetry derives three-dimensional structure by mathematically identifying common conjugate tie-points across multiple overlapping optical images. Consequently, photogrammetry naturally generates a Digital Surface Model (DSM)—a representation of the Earth's surface that intrinsically includes the tops of all natural and man-made objects, such as forest canopies, buildings, and vehicles.5 If the terrain is heavily vegetated, optical photogrammetry cannot physically penetrate the canopy to image the ground below, resulting in a model that is heavily biased toward the top-of-canopy with very little actual ground data.31 Attempting to derive a bare-earth DEM from a photogrammetric DSM in a forested area requires massive spatial interpolation and introduces significant vertical noise and uncertainty.31

LiDAR, conversely, operates as an active sensor. By emitting hundreds of thousands of rapid pulses of laser light per second, a single pulse can penetrate small gaps in foliage, resulting in multiple discrete returns per pulse, where the final return typically represents the bare ground.5 Airborne LiDAR is therefore universally considered the prime method for generating large-scale, highly accurate Digital Terrain Models (DTMs) or bare-earth DEMs.6

However, the raw LiDAR point cloud is not intrinsically a DEM; it must still undergo rigorous, computationally intense filtering algorithms to classify and remove points representing vegetation and structures.6 Simple slope-based filtering methods often fail in highly complex topography. Modern algorithms frequently combine both the range information and the intensity of the laser return to accurately segment the ground from low-lying vegetation.6 In these complex workflows, the absolute accuracy of the final filtered DEM is inextricably linked to the initial GNSS trajectory correction provided by the CORS network. Any vertical error introduced during the CORS-to-rover GNSS post-processing phase propagates geometrically directly into every single point within the resulting DEM.10

## **Error Propagation, Baseline Dynamics, and Network Real-Time Kinematics (NRTK)**

The use of CORS data for DEM generation and ground control surveying is an incredibly powerful tool, but it is bound by the physical limitations of atmospheric physics. The fundamental principle of differential GNSS is that atmospheric conditions and orbital errors are strongly spatially correlated; the closer a moving rover (or a temporary field base station) is to the reference CORS, the more identical the signal delays are, allowing for near-perfect mathematical cancellation of the errors through double-differencing.33 As the distance between the CORS station and the project site—known as the baseline length—increases, this vital spatial correlation degrades rapidly.

### **Distance-Dependent Errors and the Limits of PPK**

The propagation of distance-dependent errors is a primary, overriding concern when utilizing CORS for establishing vertical control for DEMs. The greater the baseline distance, the more likely the differential solution will be influenced by localized high-frequency ionospheric noise and tropospheric anomalies, thereby lowering the vertical accuracy of the final coordinates.33 In traditional Post-Processed Kinematic (PPK) and Real-Time Kinematic (RTK) surveying from a single base station, vertical accuracy generally degrades at a rate of roughly 1 part per million (ppm) relative to the baseline length, creating an expanding cone of uncertainty around the CORS station.35 Furthermore, when baseline distances exceed certain thresholds, it becomes computationally difficult for the processing software to reliably resolve the integer ambiguities, dropping the solution from a highly precise "fixed" state to a much less reliable "float" state.30

To mitigate these baseline distance errors over large geographic areas and ensure homogeneous DEM accuracy, the geospatial industry developed the concept of Network RTK (NRTK). Instead of relying on a single, distant CORS station, NRTK systems—such as those utilized by State Departments of Transportation and research institutions—utilize raw measurements from an entire array of surrounding CORS stations simultaneously.33 By continuously modeling the ionospheric and tropospheric delays across the regional network in real-time, the system generates a Virtual Reference Station (VRS) or applies Areal Stream Corrections (such as FKP or MAC) immediately adjacent to the user's project site.33

This advanced approach significantly mitigates distance-dependent error propagation, providing a far more homogeneous, precise vertical solution across wide areas.30 Academic literature indicates that when network methodologies are applied properly to GNSS observations, taking multiple independent observations over distinct temporal windows, network accuracies of 1.8 cm in the vertical dimension and 1.0 cm in the horizontal dimension at a 95% confidence level are completely achievable.39 This level of precision meets rigorous NGS standards (such as NOS NGS-58 and NOS NGS-59) and is entirely sufficient for serving as highly accurate ground truth for DEM evaluation.39

| NRTK Correction Method | Mechanism of Action | Distribution of Residual Errors |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Virtual Reference Station (VRS)** | Generates a fictitious base station immediately adjacent to the rover using network interpolation. | Punctual correction; studies show a tendency toward multimodal error distributions under certain atmospheric conditions.33 |
| **Flächen-Korrektur-Parameter (FKP)** | Broadcasts planar correction parameters representing the atmospheric delay gradient over the network. | Areal stream correction; generally confirms a normal distribution of positioning errors, highly reliable for broad DEM control.38 |
| **Master-Auxiliary Concept (MAC)** | Transmits raw observations from a master station and coordinate differences from auxiliary stations. | Areal stream correction; confirms a normal distribution of errors, providing a highly rigorous mathematical solution.38 |

### **Environmental Obstructions, Multipath, and Surface Mass Loading**

However, the efficacy of CORS-based corrections is heavily dictated by the immediate physical environment of the rover or the control point being established. In urban environments or dense forests, physical obstructions create signal multipath—a phenomenon where the GNSS satellite signal bounces off buildings or tree trunks before reaching the antenna, causing the signal to take a longer path than the direct line-of-sight.35 This heavily corrupts the carrier phase measurement. Studies evaluating the positional accuracy of CORS-VRS and PPK techniques in obstructed urban areas have demonstrated significant vulnerability, with baseline distance errors expanding unpredictably between 10 cm and 34 cm in the vertical component.35

For the establishment of definitive vertical ground control used to evaluate large-scale DEMs, relying solely on rapid RTK or kinematic observations in obstructed environments is profoundly insufficient. Instead, static GNSS positioning is required. By occupying a control point for extended periods (ranging from 2 to 24 hours) and post-processing the data against surrounding CORS stations, the random noise associated with multipath and atmospheric fluctuations is effectively averaged out.36

Furthermore, advanced geodetic research demonstrates that maintaining a regional elevation datum using CORS requires accounting for minute, dynamic forces previously ignored in standard topographic mapping. The vertical coordinates of CORS stations themselves are subject to variations caused by surface mass loading—including seasonal changes in land water storage, localized sea-level fluctuations, and atmospheric pressure loading.42 Utilizing a remove-calculate-restore method and Green's function integration, scientists can derive and correct the height anomaly variation of CORS stations caused by these mass loadings, with the influence reaching the sub-centimeter level.42 In regions prone to extreme subsidence, dynamically maintaining the elevation datum using these modeled mass loading corrections allows CORS networks to achieve a mean normal height accuracy of 2.7 mm, surpassing the requirements of second-class leveling surveys.42

## **Evaluating DEM Vertical Accuracy: Methodologies and Global Datasets**

The proliferation of open-source global DEMs has revolutionized earth sciences, hydrology, and global infrastructure planning, but utilizing these models responsibly requires a rigorous understanding of their vertical uncertainties. Because CORS stations are geographically dispersed, continuously monitored, and deeply tied to authoritative vertical datums, they represent the ultimate absolute ground truth for validating remote sensing elevation models at a macro scale.7

### **Absolute vs. Relative Vertical Accuracy**

When formally assessing DEMs, a critical distinction must be drawn between relative and absolute vertical accuracy. Relative accuracy measures point-to-point vertical relationships within the dataset itself (e.g., determining if the specific height difference between a hilltop and an adjacent valley floor is correct).7 This internal consistency is crucial for deriving secondary topographic indices via neighborhood parameters, such as slope gradient, aspect, and hydrological flow accumulation.7

Absolute vertical accuracy, however, accounts for all systematic and random errors, mathematically relating the modeled elevation of every single pixel directly to the true elevation established by an official global or regional vertical geodetic datum.7 Validating absolute accuracy requires comparing the DEM against an independent, significantly higher-accuracy dataset, which is precisely where CORS station coordinates are deployed.

### **Representativeness and Reference Sources**

A significant challenge in using discrete points like CORS stations to validate a coarse-resolution DEM (such as a 30m or 90m pixel) is the concept of spatial representativeness.44 If a CORS station sits on a small localized hill inside a 30m x 30m pixel that is otherwise entirely flat, the GNSS measurement is highly accurate but not representative of the average elevation of that pixel. To combat representativeness errors, researchers increasingly rely on highly rigorous spaceborne datasets, such as the newly launched ICESat-2 satellite, to supplement terrestrial CORS data. The ICESat-2 carries the Advanced Topographic Laser Altimeter System (ATLAS), emitting a 532 nm laser to provide highly accurate elevation photon data globally.45 Using the ATL08 (land and vegetation) and ATL06 (land ice) data products, researchers have generated massive datasets of ground control points. When validated against high-precision CORS measurements, ICESat-2 data demonstrates extraordinary vertical accuracy (Root Mean Square Error of \~0.08 m), making it a highly reliable secondary reference source for evaluating coarser global DEMs.45

### **Performance of Global DEMs Against CORS Ground Truth**

Extensive global studies utilizing CORS stations and precise GNSS checkpoints have yielded critical insights into the performance of popular DEM datasets. The evaluation methodology typically involves extracting the elevation value of the DEM pixel that geographically coincides with the known latitude and longitude of a CORS station, and calculating the residual difference (the error) against the station's known orthometric height.12

Results consistently indicate that radar-based DEMs generally outperform optical-based DEMs in terms of vertical stability and accuracy across varied terrain.9 The Shuttle Radar Topography Mission (SRTM) DEM (30m resolution) and the newer TerraSAR-X add-on for Digital Elevation Measurement (TanDEM-X) (90m resolution) are frequently cited as the most robust open-source options.9 TanDEM-X exhibits excellent stability, while SRTM remains the most widely utilized dataset globally due to its balance of high spatial resolution paired with high vertical accuracy.9

The Advanced Land Observing Satellite World 3D (AW3D30) DEM also performs admirably, often matching or slightly exceeding SRTM in specific flatland regions, with a Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) hovering consistently between 2.3 and 2.4 meters.10 Conversely, the Advanced Spaceborne Thermal Emission and Reflection Radiometer (ASTER) Global DEM (GDEM) consistently ranks as the least accurate among the major models, displaying higher noise and larger RMSE values, particularly over complex terrain.10

### **The Impact of Land Cover and Heteroscedastic Errors**

A vital nuance in DEM evaluation is understanding that errors in terrain models are heteroscedastic—meaning they are not distributed homogeneously across the map, but rather vary significantly based on localized, interconnected factors, most notably topographic slope and land cover.9 When using a CORS station or a GNSS rover in an open field, the coordinate genuinely reflects the true bare earth. However, if a DEM is evaluated in an area covered by dense forest canopy, the resulting vertical residual will be massive.9

Both SRTM (which utilizes a C-band radar) and ASTER (which utilizes an optical sensor) fail to penetrate thick forest canopies completely.9 Consequently, the elevation recorded by the DEM in these areas is closer to the top of the canopy rather than the bare ground.9 Research explicitly reveals that DEM accuracy decreases exponentially as terrain slope increases, as even a slight horizontal positional error translates directly into a massive vertical error on a steep face.10 Furthermore, accuracy systematically degrades as land cover transitions from non-vegetated areas (bare soil, urban surfaces) to vegetated areas (grassland, forest).9 Rectifying the systematic DEM deviation caused by forest canopy height remains a profound challenge in geospatial sciences, requiring complex algorithmic interventions to estimate true "bare-earth" elevations beneath the vegetation prior to utilizing the DEM in precision applications.9

## **The Evolution of ASPRS Positional Accuracy Standards**

To mathematically standardize how the geospatial industry measures, evaluates, and reports the errors discussed above, the American Society for Photogrammetry and Remote Sensing (ASPRS) publishes strict, consensus-based guidelines. The release of the ASPRS Positional Accuracy Standards for Digital Geospatial Data (Edition 2, Version 2.0) introduced profound modifications to how DEM vertical accuracy is assessed using independent ground control, specifically GNSS observations tied to the CORS network.13

### **The Shift to Root Mean Square Error (RMSE)**

The most significant philosophical shift in Edition 2 was the complete elimination of the 95% confidence level as the primary accuracy measure, a metric that had been carried forward from the legacy 1998 National Standard for Spatial Data Accuracy (NSSDA).50 Historically, reporting two separate quantities representing the same accuracy—the Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) and the 95% confidence interval—created profound confusion among data producers and consumers, as both were derived from the same error distribution.51 Edition 2 mandates the use of ![][image3] (Root Mean Square Error in the vertical direction) or ![][image4] as the sole, reliable statistical term to express vertical product accuracy, operating under the formal assumption that vertical errors are normally distributed, the sample size is sufficiently large, and the mean error is sufficiently small.13

### **Stratification of Accuracy: NVA and VVA**

Addressing the specific physical limitations of LiDAR and photogrammetry in vegetated areas as explored previously, the ASPRS standard maintains a strict bifurcation in vertical accuracy reporting based on land cover classification:

* **Non-vegetated Vertical Accuracy (NVA):** Evaluated exclusively in open, unobscured terrain where sensors have a direct line-of-sight to the ground. This metric serves as the primary baseline health indicator of the geometric trajectory and sensor calibration.  
* **Vegetated Vertical Accuracy (VVA):** Evaluated specifically in areas where the terrain is obscured by vegetation. Because vegetation causes unpredictable scattering of LiDAR pulses and heavily blocks GNSS signals for ground surveyors establishing checkpoints, errors here are often skewed and not normally distributed. Notably, Edition 2 removed the strict pass/fail requirement for VVA, legally acknowledging the immense environmental variability that makes guaranteeing a specific accuracy under dense canopy practically impossible for data providers.13

### **Integrating Survey Error into Final Product Accuracy**

Perhaps the most critical technical update in Edition 2 relates to the physical checkpoints themselves. In previous iterations (Edition 1), ground checkpoints were required to be three to four times more accurate than the intended final map product.51 As modern UAV and LiDAR DEMs routinely achieve vertical accuracies of a few centimeters, achieving checkpoints three times more accurate using standard RTK/NRTK methodologies tied to CORS became physically and economically impossible, barring the use of excessively slow and costly precise digital leveling.51

Edition 2 relaxed these overly stringent checkpoint accuracy requirements, recognizing the logistical burden they placed on GNSS field surveying practices.51 However, the standard introduced a mathematically rigorous caveat: the intrinsic vertical error of the GNSS survey used to measure the checkpoint must now be explicitly and transparently included when computing the final accuracy of the DEM.13

## **Dynamic Datums, Tectonics, and the California Case Study**

A profound, yet frequently misunderstood element of DEM generation and evaluation is the temporal nature of geodetic coordinates. Geospatial professionals often treat coordinates as immutable facts. However, the Earth's crust is highly dynamic; tectonic plates constantly shift, and phenomena such as subsidence from groundwater extraction or post-seismic relaxation continuously alter the topography.42 When relying on the CORS network to generate or evaluate a DEM, one is not merely dealing in X, Y, and Z spatial dimensions, but crucially, a fourth dimension: time.

Nowhere is this dynamic spatial environment more evident or problematic than in California, where the tectonic interplay between the Pacific and North American plates creates constant, massive crustal deformation.55 Base stations in California shift laterally between 10 and 50 millimeters annually, and sometimes shift by significantly more during acute seismic events.15 If the CORS coordinates defining the base stations are not constantly updated, the GNSS solutions they provide will be fundamentally flawed.

To systematically manage this volatility, the California Spatial Reference Center (CSRC) has the codified authority (via California Public Resources Code Sections 8850-8861) to compute and publish coordinates for the California Spatial Reference Network (CSRN), which archives data from over 850 continuous GNSS stations.22 The CSRC publishes a dynamic datum, such as the "CSRS Epoch 2017.50 (NAD83)," which realizes geodetic coordinates explicitly frozen at a specific moment in time (in this case, July 2, 2017).14 Commercial RTK networks operating within the state, such as SmartNet, conduct semi-annual adjustments (e.g., Spring.250 and Fall.750 epochs) to keep their broadcast coordinates continuously aligned with the shifting ground reality while rigorously maintaining mathematical ties to the national NSRS standard.55

### **The Danger of "No-Epoch" Data in DEM Differencing**

The concept of the coordinate epoch is critical when evaluating terrain change over time, a vital engineering process known as creating DEMs of Difference (DoD).47 DoDs are generated by mathematically subtracting a historical DEM from a current DEM to measure volumetric changes on the Earth's surface, such as assessing floodplain erosion, monitoring landslide displacement, or calculating construction site earthwork excavation.5

If an engineer creates a DEM in 2015 utilizing CORS data, and then creates a subsequent DEM in 2025 without strictly aligning the spatial epoch metadata, the entire DEM will be shifted by the cumulative tectonic drift of the region. In a highly active tectonic zone like California, a 10-year gap equates to nearly half a meter of horizontal displacement.15 If these two "No-Epoch" DEMs are subtracted from one another without temporal alignment, the lateral misalignment along sloping terrain will manifest instantly as massive, entirely fictitious vertical elevation changes.10 As noted extensively in professional surveying literature, providing or utilizing coordinates without a designated epoch immediately injects an unknown spatial shift into the data, guaranteeing that the terrain models will not accurately map over each other.15 Accurate temporal modeling demands that both the historical and contemporary DEMs are processed, translated, and transformed into the exact same epoch frame using dynamic velocity models before any volumetric subtraction or analysis occurs.15

## **Utilizing NGS OPUS Projects for DEM Ground Control Validation**

To navigate the immense complexities of datums, epochs, and baseline error propagation, geospatial professionals rely heavily on specialized, highly regulated software to establish the absolute vertical ground control used to generate or validate DEMs. The premier tool provided by the NGS for this rigorous purpose is OPUS Projects (Online Positioning User Service).41

OPUS Projects allows users to upload multiple static GNSS observation files collected at project checkpoints over extended periods and process them collectively as an interconnected, localized network rather than as isolated points.41 The software automatically queries the national database to locate the most appropriate, stable CORS stations surrounding the project site to serve as the master control hubs.58 By utilizing the sophisticated PAGES software suite underlying the interface, OPUS Projects performs a rigorous least-squares network adjustment, mitigating atmospheric anomalies, ionospheric delays, and multipath errors far more effectively than isolated single-baseline processing ever could.41 Modern updates to the platform also allow for the uploading of GVX (GNSS Vector Exchange) vectors directly from kinematic field surveys, further integrating RTK data with static CORS observations.58

Furthermore, OPUS Projects seamlessly handles the underlying datum mechanics that routinely plague DEM creation. It mathematically aligns the newly surveyed DEM control points strictly to the National Spatial Reference System (NSRS), explicitly accounting for reference frames and epoch dates.14 For large-scale infrastructure and flood modeling DEMs, utilizing OPUS Projects to establish the primary ground control network ensures that the final topographic product is legally defensible, geodetically sound, and fully interoperable with state and federal datasets.59 Survey projects that meet strict rigorous criteria can be formally submitted through OPUS Projects for inclusion in the NGS Integrated Database (IDB), a process historically known as "Bluebooking," ensuring the control marks used for the DEM become permanent, authoritative additions to the national geodetic framework.57

## **Pedagogical Frameworks for Geodesy and DEM Analysis**

The transition of geodesy from traditional, line-of-sight surveying—utilizing optical levels and theodolites to establish elevation—to a complex, digital toolbox dominated by GNSS, LiDAR, and Structure-from-Motion (SfM) presents a profound challenge for academic instruction.16 To effectively educate undergraduate students, graduate students, and working professionals on the intricate relationship between the CORS network and DEM accuracy, educators must employ highly targeted, scaffolded pedagogical strategies that align with modern STEM curricula.16

The integration of advanced geodesy into educational frameworks, such as the evolution seen in the Department of Geodesy in Kosovo through the Tempus Project Master's Program in Geodesy (MPG) operating under the Bologna system, demonstrates that curricula must be highly adaptive to technological shifts and labor market demands.17 Modern pedagogy must move beyond rote memorization of surveying formulas and embrace dynamic problem-solving.

### **Undergraduate Education: Establishing the Mental Model**

At the undergraduate level, students often lack the complex mathematical maturity to intuitively grasp non-geocentric ellipsoids, atmospheric refraction models, or least-squares adjustments. Therefore, the pedagogical goal should be experiential learning that establishes a strong, intuitive conceptual mental model.16 Curricula should prioritize hands-on field courses, utilizing extensive open-source teaching collections like GETSI (GEophysics Tools for Societal Issues).16

Undergraduates should operate GNSS rover equipment to physically collect ground control points, followed by basic UAV flight operations over a designated test site.16 Instruction must explicitly bridge the gap between the invisible satellite signals orbiting the Earth and the physical terrain under their feet. By having undergraduates process their raw field data through a simplified interface like standard NGS OPUS, they can observe how connecting their local, drifting rover measurements to a distant, hyper-stable CORS station instantly alters and tightens the positional uncertainty of their coordinates. Assessing student learning at this foundational stage should focus heavily on their ability to explain the *why*—why the differential correction is necessary, and why raw GPS yields a poor DEM—rather than forcing them to compute the intricate mathematics of the adjustment.16

### **Graduate Education: Algorithm Optimization and Non-Linear Error Propagation**

Graduate students, conversely, require a far deeper, highly quantitative approach. The focus must shift radically toward critical analysis, algorithmic optimization, and managing complex error propagation through multi-stage geospatial workflows.32 Pedagogical frameworks at this level should incorporate project-based learning involving massive, raw, unprocessed datasets.

Graduate curricula should require students to manually process airborne GNSS/IMU trajectories utilizing downloaded CORS RINEX data in scientific software like Applanix POSPac.29 They must analyze the degradation of the trajectory solution when forced to use CORS baselines that are deliberately extended beyond acceptable limits (e.g., comparing a 10km baseline solution to a 150km baseline solution) to witness the collapse of the vertical accuracy and the subsequent distortion of the DEM firsthand.33

Furthermore, graduate education is the appropriate venue to heavily emphasize algorithmic bare-earth filtering of LiDAR point clouds.6 Students must code and test algorithms that parse both range and intensity data to strip vegetation from DEMs.6 Finally, they must master the mathematical calculation of non-linear error propagation—utilizing advanced methods such as Monte Carlo simulations and the Scaled unscented transformation—to accurately model how errors in the CORS baseline propagate into the final terrain model, ensuring they fully understand the rigorous statistical validation of DEM products dictated by ASPRS Edition 2\.13

### **Professional Development: Policy, Datums, and Regulatory Compliance**

For continuing education and professional development, the audience consists of licensed surveyors, civil engineers, and GIS managers. These individuals generally already understand the mechanics of field data collection; their educational needs are centered entirely on risk management, regulatory compliance, and integrating massive new paradigm shifts into their existing corporate or governmental workflows.17

Instruction for professionals must be deeply anchored in policy and best practices, such as those published by Caltrans for integrating mobile LiDAR and photogrammetry using CORS to eliminate physical base stations.27 Seminars must focus heavily on the tectonic implications of spatial data, teaching the profound legal and engineering necessity of tracking Epoch metadata (e.g., CSRS Epoch 2017.50) to prevent catastrophic engineering failures when utilizing multi-temporal DEMs of Difference.14

Most urgently, current professional instruction must focus relentlessly on the transition to the modernized 2022 NSRS. Training must center on the practical realities of abandoning NAVD 88 for NAPGD 2022, utilizing the NGS Coordinate Conversion and Transformation Tool (NCAT) and VERTCON 3.0 to update legacy infrastructure DEMs, and navigating the nuances of the new State Plane Coordinate System of 2022 (SPCS2022).21

## **Conclusion**

The creation of an accurate Digital Elevation Model is a multi-disciplinary triumph that seamlessly merges aerospace technology, advanced optical and laser physics, and profound geographic theory. Yet, regardless of how advanced a UAV camera or an airborne LiDAR sensor may be, the resulting topographic model is entirely meaningless without a rigid, mathematically robust connection to the Earth's geodetic framework. The Continuously Operating Reference Station (CORS) Network serves as this critical, indispensable anchor. By providing the essential GNSS data necessary for post-processed kinematic trajectories, CORS infrastructure effectively neutralizes the immense orbital and atmospheric errors inherent in satellite navigation, distilling positional uncertainty down from meters to the centimeter scale.

Furthermore, as the global scientific community increasingly relies on massive open-source terrain datasets to solve pressing environmental and infrastructural challenges, CORS stations provide the indispensable, absolute ground-truth necessary to evaluate their viability. The evolution of ASPRS positional accuracy standards reflects a maturing industry that now openly mathematically acknowledges the limitations of sensor capabilities under dense vegetation and demands total transparency regarding the inherent errors of the geodetic surveys themselves.

As the geospatial industry advances rapidly toward the modernized 2022 National Spatial Reference System, abandoning flawed historical datums in favor of dynamic, active, satellite-derived reference frames, reliance on the CORS network will only intensify. Ensuring the integrity of future topographical applications—ranging from autonomous vehicle navigation to predictive climate flood modeling—will demand a highly educated new generation of practitioners. Through carefully scaffolded pedagogical frameworks that address the underlying mathematics of error propagation and the critical realities of tectonic epoch shifting, educators can ensure that the geospatial professionals of tomorrow are fully equipped to translate the raw signals of the CORS network into the definitive topographic models of the future.
