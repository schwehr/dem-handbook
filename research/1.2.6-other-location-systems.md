# **1.2.6 Other Location Systems: Radio Beacons, Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, Cell Towers, LORAN, and Emerging Terrestrial PNT**

## **Introduction: The Geodetic Imperative for Non-GNSS Positioning**

The integrity of any modern Digital Elevation Model (DEM) is fundamentally dependent upon the precise spatial referencing of the sensors utilized to measure the Earth's surface. While Global Navigation Satellite Systems (GNSS), including constellations such as GPS, GLONASS, Galileo, and BeiDou, remain the undisputed backbone of modern geodetic positioning, their reliance on ultra-low-power microwave signals transmitted from medium Earth orbit inherently limits their operational envelope.1 GNSS signals are exceptionally susceptible to attenuation by physical barriers, completely blocked in subterranean or underwater environments, heavily degraded by multipath effects in dense urban canyons, and increasingly vulnerable to intentional electromagnetic interference, including localized jamming and sophisticated spoofing attacks.3

When a mobile mapping platform—whether an uncrewed aerial vehicle (UAV) executing a photogrammetry grid, a subterranean robotic rover navigating a mine shaft, or a shallow-water hydrographic vessel surveying a fluvial system—loses its GNSS lock, the subsequent elevation data loses its spatial anchor.7 Without an alternative, highly reliable positioning mechanism, the resulting DEM suffers from severe geometric distortion, catastrophic absolute positioning errors, and uncorrectable trajectory drift.9 Furthermore, as autonomous navigation systems and highly precise mapping requirements converge, the necessity for robust, redundant Position, Navigation, and Timing (PNT) architectures has become paramount.3

Consequently, the field of geodesy has experienced a massive resurgence in the development, refinement, and integration of alternative, terrestrial-based location systems.12 These systems leverage diverse segments of the electromagnetic spectrum, ranging from low-frequency ground waves capable of penetrating dense physical barriers to ultra-high-frequency optical and acoustic pulses designed for micro-positioning in highly localized environments.13 This comprehensive analysis examines the technical architectures, positional accuracies, and specific geodetic applications of alternative location systems utilized in DEM generation. The examination begins with the historical marine hyperbolic systems that laid the foundation for modern electronic surveying, transitions into the modern resurgence of high-power low-frequency terrestrial systems like Enhanced LORAN (eLORAN), explores the rapidly advancing domain of 5G cellular positioning, and dissects the local-area radio frequency (RF) networks that enable high-fidelity subterranean and indoor topographic mapping. Finally, the analysis explores the unifying mathematical framework of sensor fusion, which integrates these disparate modalities into a cohesive, continuous trajectory for the modern mapping sensor.13

## **1\. The Historical Foundation: Hyperbolic and Phase-Comparison Marine Systems**

Long before the advent of satellite navigation, the absolute imperative to generate accurate bathymetric DEMs, nautical charts, and coastal inundation models drove the development of complex terrestrial radio positioning networks.17 Understanding these legacy systems is critical for modern geodesists, not merely as historical context, but because their foundational mathematics—specifically hyperbolic multilateration and phase-comparison continuous-wave signal processing—remain directly applicable to modern 5G and localized terrestrial networks.19 Furthermore, millions of historical depth soundings retained in modern global datasets, such as the General Bathymetric Chart of the Oceans (GEBCO), were georeferenced utilizing these legacy systems, meaning their specific error budgets must be mathematically accounted for when harmonizing historical bathymetry with modern LiDAR or multibeam sonar DEMs.18

### **1.1 Decca, Hi-Fix, and Raydist Architectures**

In the mid-to-late 20th century, high-resolution hydrographic surveying relied heavily on medium-range terrestrial radio navigation systems.21 The Decca positioning system operated by utilizing continuous wave transmissions and relied heavily on the phase-comparison method of measurement.21 While early general-navigation Decca systems broadcasted in the low-frequency 70 to 130 kHz range with four frequencies per system, specialized high-resolution surveying variants operated as hyperbolic phase-comparison systems in the 2 MHz band.21 Decca could be configured in a Hyperbolic Plot mode, where master and slave stations located ashore allowed an unlimited number of maritime users to navigate simultaneously, or a Circular Plot mode, commonly referred to as Two-Range Decca.21 In the Two-Range configuration, the master station was placed directly on board the survey vessel itself, creating circular phase patterns that permitted significantly greater geometric accuracy over an extended operational area, although it limited the network to a single surveying user at any given time.21 The standard resolution of this system was remarkably fine for its era, achieving precision down to 0.01 of a phase lane, with a maximum operational range of approximately 300 miles (500 kilometers).21

For higher-resolution bathymetric mapping, specialized hydrographic systems such as Hi-Fix and Raydist were frequently employed by agencies conducting precision fluvial and coastal mapping.21 Hi-Fix, developed by the Decca Company, operated as a phase-comparison, hyperbolic system in the 2 MHz band with a maximum operational range of approximately 150 miles.21 Unlike standard continuous-wave Decca, Hi-Fix utilized a pulsed transmission methodology.21 This system was widely adopted by organizations such as the United States Naval Oceanographic Office (USNOO) and the United States Coast and Geodetic Survey (USC\&GS) for detailed coastal work, and miniaturized versions, such as Sea-Fix, were even deployed directly onto transponder buoys for localized relative positioning.23

In direct contrast to Hi-Fix, the Raydist system utilized a continuous wave architecture requiring four discrete radio channels, with two channels strictly required to be harmonically related, to achieve true two-range positioning.21 Because Raydist utilized continuous waves rather than isolated pulses, it was highly sensitive to minute spatial deviations; the system was so geometrically precise that the physical rolling, pitching, and yawing of the hydrographic survey vessel could be directly observed as oscillations on the phase indicators.21 By doubling its fundamental frequency compared to the Hi-Fix system, Raydist reduced its phase lane-widths by half, thereby achieving a spatial resolution twice as fine.21

### **1.2 Fluvial Bathymetry and Legacy Error Propagation**

These systems were instrumental in mapping dynamic riverine environments. For example, historical acoustic surveys conducted by the US Army Corps of Engineers (USACE) and the United States Geological Survey (USGS) on critical waterways like the Sacramento River in California relied heavily on terrestrial radio positioning before the advent of RTK GNSS.8 The Sacramento District of the USACE utilized extensive networks of local shore stations and early radio beacons to maintain positional control while gathering depth soundings, mapping complex fluvial geomorphology across hundreds of miles of the river.8 Modern topo-bathymetric surveys of these same river reaches—now utilizing airborne topographic and bathymetric LiDAR paired with continuous RTK GNSS and IMU sensor fusion—must often compare their updated 1-meter resolution DEMs against these historical radio-navigated datasets to model morphological changes, sediment transport, and habitat evolution.24

However, integrating this legacy data requires an advanced understanding of the systemic error budgets inherent to medium and high-frequency radio surveys. In these legacy systems, positional accuracy was not defined merely by a static spatial tolerance, but rather by "resolution" (the smallest measurable spatial increment) and "repeatability" (the consistency of the receiver returning to the exact same phase lane reading for the same fixed geographic point).21 Commercial electronic distance measuring equipment (DME) of this era was calibrated at highly standardized atmospheric conditions; consequently, natural fluctuations in barometric pressure, ambient temperature, and relative humidity over the surveyed water body caused minor shifts in the propagation velocity of the radio waves, introducing systemic range errors.22

Furthermore, high-frequency systems like Hi-Fix and Raydist were severely affected by skywave interference during nocturnal operations, where signals refracting off the ionosphere would mix with the desired groundwave, causing severe phase ambiguity and catastrophic positioning jumps.23 During daytime operations, their maximum range was often severely limited by atmospheric radio noise.28 To mitigate these issues in the resulting bathymetric DEMs, hydrographers had to conduct frequent physical calibrations using known terrestrial transit fixes and manually apply complex geometric corrections for the vessel's speed, as the phase comparison sequence required approximately one full second to cycle—a significant temporal lag that induced along-track positioning errors for any vessel surveying at high speed.18

## **2\. Aeronautical Radio Beacons and Terrestrial Ground Control**

Concurrently with marine developments, national airspaces and terrestrial mapping networks were governed by vast arrays of aeronautical radio beacons. While designed primarily for aerial navigation, the physical infrastructure of these beacons served as critical geodetic control points (benchmarks) for terrestrial surveyors establishing local datums and referencing early topographic DEMs.29

### **2.1 The Evolution of the Airway Beacon Network**

In the early decades of the 20th century, commercial aviation and airmail services relied upon massive concrete arrows on the ground, illuminated by light beacons, to guide pilots across transcontinental routes.30 Constructed by the Department of Commerce in the 1920s and 1930s, over 1,500 of these visual beacons were spaced 10 to 20 miles apart.30 With the advent of instrument flight rules, this visual network was rapidly supplanted by the Low Frequency Radio Range (LFR) system, which established a complex network of over 881 stations across North America transmitting directional audio signals.32 Concurrently, massive naval communication infrastructures, such as the US Naval Radio Station at Mare Island and the historic KPH Maritime Radio Receiving Station operating near Bolinas and the Point Reyes coastline in California, established immense low-frequency radio footprints that could be utilized for rudimentary direction-finding by offshore survey vessels mapping the complex bathymetry of the Pacific continental shelf.33 Inland, installations spanning from the Sutter Buttes to Live Oak, California, further densified the local radio topography, serving dual purposes for military communications and early localized triangulation efforts.25

### **2.2 VOR, NDB, and Precision Distance Measuring Equipment (DME)**

The modern era of terrestrial radio navigation is defined by Very High Frequency Omnidirectional Range (VOR) stations, Non-Directional Beacons (NDB), and Distance Measuring Equipment (DME).39 NDBs are elementary radio beacons operating in the low to medium frequency bands that allow basic direction-finding equipment to home in on a station.41 VOR systems provide a highly reliable 360-degree radial bearing projecting outward from the station, while DME provides highly accurate slant-range distance measurements utilizing rapid pulsed interrogations and replies.39

In the context of ground surveying and regional topographic mapping, the absolute positional accuracy of these legacy networks varies significantly based on the hardware standard implemented. Legacy DME specifications generally guarantee 95% ranging accuracies of approximately 315 meters (0.17 nautical miles), while advanced Precision DME (DME/P) installations can tighten this spatial tolerance down to an impressive 15 meters.42 While many global civil aviation authorities are actively decommissioning legacy NDBs and older VOR infrastructure in favor of GNSS-based Area Navigation (RNAV), optimized networks of ground-based DME/DME transponders are actually being retained, modernized, and geographically densified.43

By meticulously modeling and optimizing the geographic topology of a DME network across a regional landscape, authorities can ensure positioning coverage gaps are no larger than 0.3 nautical miles (approximately 555 meters), or even drive continuous positioning accuracy down to 92.6 meters in highly optimized configurations.42 For uncrewed aerial vehicles (UAVs) conducting broad-area photogrammetry in environments where GNSS signals are actively spoofed, a multi-DME positioning array serves as an ultimate, un-jammable terrestrial fallback, ensuring the aerial platform maintains sufficient spatial awareness to prevent total trajectory loss and safely complete its mapping traverse.42 Furthermore, the exact geographic coordinates of these massive beacon antennas are rigorously monitored and maintained by national mapping agencies, such as the National Geodetic Survey (NGS), serving as primary physical ground control points for broader terrestrial leveling and surveying campaigns.29

## **3\. The Resurgence of Low-Frequency Resilient PNT: LORAN and eLORAN**

The Long Range Navigation (LORAN) system, originating in World War II as LORAN-A and later evolving into the ubiquitous LORAN-C standard, was a hyperbolic radio navigation system that defined long-distance maritime positioning for several decades.17 By precisely measuring the time difference between synchronized radio pulses transmitted from a vast chain of terrestrial stations, maritime and aerial navigators could plot intersecting hyperbolic lines of position to achieve horizontal accuracies of hundreds of feet.12 Although the United States and Canadian LORAN-C systems were officially decommissioned in 2010 due to the ubiquitous dominance and perceived infallibility of GPS, the rapidly escalating threat of GNSS vulnerabilities—ranging from solar weather anomalies to state-sponsored jamming—has catalyzed the intensive development and deployment of Enhanced LORAN (eLORAN).48

### **3.1 The Physics, Architecture, and Timing Precision of eLORAN**

Operating at a significantly low frequency of approximately 100 kHz, eLORAN signals propagate primarily as ground waves that travel directly along the Earth's surface contour.1 This physical propagation characteristic grants eLORAN two profound geodetic advantages over satellite-based GNSS. First, the transmission power is immense; an average eLORAN signal is over 1.3 million times stronger than a standard GNSS signal upon reaching the surface receiver.52 This renders the system highly resilient against intentional radio frequency interference (RFI) and effectively immune to localized, low-power jamming devices that easily overwhelm GPS receivers.1 Second, the 100 kHz groundwave exhibits profound physical penetration capabilities, successfully reaching receivers deep within dense urban canyons, inside heavy concrete structures, and even in shallow underground or underwater surveying environments where microwave GNSS signals are instantly absorbed or reflected.1

The modern eLORAN architecture drastically upgrades the legacy LORAN-C infrastructure by integrating advanced solid-state transmitters, ultra-precise Cesium atomic clocks, and a dedicated, integrated data channel utilizing Pulse Phase Modulation (PPM).51 This data channel continuously broadcasts differential corrections, functionally similar to terrestrial Differential GPS (DGPS) or Satellite-Based Augmentation Systems (SBAS), significantly enhancing both timing and positioning precision.17 In precision timing applications—which are absolutely fundamental to synchronizing dispersed mobile mapping sensors such as LiDAR scanners and high-speed photogrammetric cameras—differential eLORAN has demonstrated the ability to distribute Coordinated Universal Time (UTC) traceable signals with accuracies better than 100 nanoseconds deep indoors.55 In highly optimized regional setups utilizing correlation methods, localized timing errors have been successfully reduced from nearly 400 nanoseconds down to less than 20 nanoseconds, providing a timing bedrock that rivals GNSS.56

### **3.2 Mitigation of Propagation Delay: The ASF Challenge**

The primary geodetic challenge in achieving survey-grade spatial accuracy with eLORAN lies in the exact prediction of its signal propagation velocity. Unlike GNSS signals, which travel through the relatively predictable vacuum of space before briefly encountering the ionosphere and troposphere, eLORAN ground waves interact continuously with the Earth's heterogeneous surface.1 The absolute time of arrival at the mapping receiver is mathematically dictated by three variables: the Primary Factor (which assumes propagation over a uniform seawater sphere), the Secondary Factor (SF, accounting for the additional delay caused by an all-seawater path relative to a perfect vacuum), and the highly variable Additional Secondary Factor (ASF).1

The ASF mathematically accounts for the precise delay induced by the low-frequency signal traveling over complex landmasses exhibiting varying electrical conductivities, undulating topographies, and shifting meteorological conditions.1 Variations in terrain elevation directly and nonlinearly influence this signal propagation delay, as do seasonal fluctuations in soil moisture and ambient temperature profiles.58 To successfully utilize eLORAN for accurate mobile mapping and DEM generation, highly localized, ultra-precise ASF spatial grids must be established. Recent advancements in spatial modeling utilize advanced machine learning algorithms, such as weighted linear regression (WLR) combined with anisotropic general regression neural networks (AGRNN), to model the immensely complex, nonlinear relationships between localized meteorological factors, prior terrain elevation data, and the resulting eLORAN/GPS time-difference (TD) delays.58 When these ASF maps are rigorously calibrated, eLORAN can achieve absolute horizontal positioning accuracies capable of supporting precise maritime harbor navigation and serving as a robust positional constraint for broad-area terrestrial mobile mapping.4

### **3.3 The Vertical Constraint: Barometric Fusion for 3D Modeling**

A critical geodetic limitation of hyperbolic terrestrial systems like eLORAN in the specific context of DEM generation is that they are inherently two-dimensional positioning systems.59 They calculate latitude and longitude with high precision but provide absolutely no vertical guidance.59 Because a Digital Elevation Model is fundamentally a representation of the Z-axis (elevation), an eLORAN-only spatial trajectory is fundamentally useless for topographic mapping.

To resolve the Z-axis in a completely GNSS-denied environment while relying on eLORAN for the X and Y coordinates, the mobile mapping platform must mathematically integrate highly sensitive barometric altimeters or inertial vertical reference units.59 Atmospheric pressure sensors, while completely immune to radio frequency interference and GNSS spoofing attacks, are subject to dynamic meteorological drift and localized pressure anomalies.45 A barometric altimeter operating in isolation can suffer from massive systemic errors; however, when fused mathematically with the stable 2D plane provided by eLORAN (and potentially further constrained by known ground control points, localized visual odometry, or intermittent GNSS availability), advanced sensor fusion algorithms can provide exceptionally tight confidence bounds on altitude measurements.60 For large-scale airborne mapping, advanced Wide Area Multilateration (WAM) architectures utilizing barometric fusion can provide continuous altitude accuracies of approximately 200 feet (99.7% confidence).54 While this vertical tolerance is insufficient for high-resolution micro-topography, it is absolutely vital for maintaining macro-level flight safety, tracking gross terrain deformation, and providing a coarse elevation model during total, prolonged GNSS blackouts.54

### **3.4 Emerging Horizons: LORAN-5G**

To bridge the technological gap between legacy high-power radio infrastructure and modern precision data requirements, researchers have proposed novel, hybrid signal designs such as LORAN-5G. This conceptual framework fundamentally replaces the legacy LORAN-C low-frequency pulses with advanced Orthogonal Frequency-Division Multiplexing (OFDM) reference signals, directly modeled after the 3GPP Release-16 standards currently utilized for 5G-NR cellular positioning.53 By utilizing a specialized Timing Reference Signal (TRS) equipped with built-in mathematical orthogonality, the LORAN-5G architecture protects the receiver against near-far signal masking issues and in-close ionospheric multipath reflections.53

Crucially, this modernized signal structure requires a peak transmission power that is two to three orders of magnitude lower than legacy LORAN-C pulses, drastically reducing both capital expenditures for power amplifiers and ongoing operational costs, while simultaneously enabling an integrated digital data channel with a raw data rate exceeding 2.6 kbps per station.53 This robust data channel broadcasts real-time station locations, highly precise reference timing, and dynamic, localized ASF correction data, pushing low-frequency terrestrial radio closer to the stringent spatial tolerances required for autonomous vehicle navigation and wide-area topographic surveying.53

## **4\. Cellular Positioning Systems: The 5G and 6G Paradigm**

The evolution of cellular positioning from a coarse emergency-services requirement (e.g., E911 triangulations utilizing basic cell-tower proximity) into a precision geodetic tool represents one of the most significant paradigm shifts in modern mobile mapping.19 While 1G and 2G networks relied on rudimentary signal strength indicators and basic cell-sector identification, the advent of 5G New Radio (NR) and the rigorous specifications set forth by the 3rd Generation Partnership Project (3GPP) have transformed the standard cellular network into a high-density, multi-modal positioning grid capable of supplementing—and in highly structured environments, substituting—GNSS for local-area DEM generation.19

### **4.1 5G Positioning Modalities and Geometry**

The modern 5G positioning architecture relies on a highly synchronized multi-Radio Access Technology (RAT) network where the precise location of the User Equipment (UE)—in the context of surveying, the mobile mapping sensor, LiDAR backpack, or autonomous drone—is calculated via rapid, continuous communication with multiple base stations or Transmission and Reception Points (TRPs).19 5G positioning definitively breaks away from the inaccuracies of simple RSSI proximity metrics by utilizing rigorous, sub-nanosecond timing and highly directional angular measurements 20:

* **Time Difference of Arrival (TDOA):** Both Downlink (DL) and Uplink (UL) TDOA protocols measure the exact time variations of radio signals arriving from multiple synchronized base stations.20 By capturing the minute time difference of the signal's arrival between pairs of TRPs, the network calculates intersecting hyperbolas in space. For a reliable 2D position calculation, a clear line-of-sight (LOS) to at least three base station antennas is mathematically required.19  
* **Angle of Arrival (AOA) and Angle of Departure (AOD):** Leveraging the massive Multiple-Input Multiple-Output (MIMO) arrays and Advanced Antenna Systems (AAS) inherent to 5G infrastructure, base stations can mathematically determine the highly specific directional beam a signal is utilizing.19 Crucially, AAS grids measure both the azimuth (horizontal plane) and the zenith (vertical plane) angles.19  
* **Multi-Round Trip Time (Multi-RTT):** This sophisticated method abandons differential timing and instead measures the absolute signal travel time back and forth between the mapping equipment and multiple base stations, creating intersecting spheres of distance to calculate an absolute spatial coordinate independent of tight inter-station clock synchronization.20  
* **Sidelink Positioning:** Introduced in newer 3GPP releases specifically to support autonomy and uncrewed vehicle swarms, Sidelink enables device-to-device relative positioning without routing data back through the cellular core network.20 Surveying drones or autonomous ground vehicles (AGVs) can utilize TDOA and power-level metrics to constantly measure distances and relative directions to one another, augmenting their absolute 5G/GNSS positions with highly accurate relative spatial clusters, essential for synchronized multi-sensor mapping missions.20

The inclusion of the zenith angle in AOA/AOD calculations is highly consequential for DEM generation, as it provides the critical Z-axis spatial coordinate (elevation) that earlier 2D cellular triangulations lacked. By accurately estimating the Angle of Arrival across both planes and calculating the exact distance to the device via time-of-flight measurements, a single robust 5G base station possesses the capability to determine a full 3D position of a surveying drone without requiring multi-tower triangulation.19

### **4.2 Accuracy Profiles and Mobile Mapping Integration**

The absolute positional accuracy of 5G networks scales dramatically based on environmental clarity, base station density, and the specific 3GPP release implemented by the network operator.19 In expansive outdoor environments, base 5G tracking utilizing standard network signaling provides positioning accuracies generally falling between 20 to 50 meters, particularly in Non-Line-of-Sight (NLOS) conditions heavily obscured by urban clutter.19 However, when a clear line-of-sight is established with AOA-equipped base stations, these spatial errors consistently drop below 20 meters.19

For the exacting spatial tolerances of high-fidelity DEM generation, 5G shines not solely as a standalone locator, but as an ultra-low-latency, high-bandwidth augmentation network. The 5G architecture seamlessly distributes Real-Time Kinematic (RTK) corrections to mapping platforms in real-time, transforming standard GNSS observations (which typically provide 2 to 10-meter accuracy) into a precision instrument capable of centimeter-level accuracy.19 Furthermore, advancements encapsulated in 3GPP Release 18 introduced precise carrier phase measurements, bandwidth aggregation, and direct GNSS augmentation into the cellular standard, driving standalone cellular accuracy deeply into the centimetric domain.19 Subsequent iterations, such as Release 19, incorporate Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Machine Learning (ML) algorithms directly at the network edge to mathematically infer and correct positioning degradation caused by severe multipath reflections in NLOS environments.19

In indoor or highly structured industrial environments—where GNSS signals are entirely denied—Release 17 of the 5G standard pushes horizontal accuracies down to 20 centimeters.19 This level of precision enables autonomous ground vehicles (AGVs) equipped with multi-modal exteroceptive sensors (such as LiDAR and stereo cameras) to conduct precise, continuous mobile scanning of indoor topographies, subterranean factory floors, or complex warehouse environments without losing their spatial anchor.19 Future 6G networks intend to expand upon these capabilities through Integrated Sensing and Communication (ISAC), which will allow the cellular network itself to act as a massive radar array, positioning non-connected objects and dynamically maintaining up-to-date digital models of dense urban environments by tracking pedestrians, vehicles, and structures simultaneously.13

## **5\. Local Area and Indoor RF Positioning: Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, and UWB**

A rapidly growing frontier in DEM generation involves the mapping of subterranean structures (such as subway tunnels or cave systems), complex building interiors, and heavily canopied forest environments where both GNSS and macro-cellular networks utterly fail to penetrate.66 In these localized domains, terrestrial mapping relies entirely on the deployment or exploitation of local area networks utilizing Wireless Fidelity (Wi-Fi), Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE), and Ultra-Wideband (UWB) radio technologies.13

### **5.1 Wi-Fi Positioning and Received Signal Strength (RSS)**

Wi-Fi infrastructures, globally pervasive in modern urban, commercial, and academic environments, provide a massive, ready-made constellation of RF reference points for spatial positioning.68 Historically, Wi-Fi positioning relied heavily on Received Signal Strength Indicator (RSSI) fingerprinting.13 Surveyors would meticulously map the RSSI of various Access Points (APs) across an environmental grid, compiling a spatial database. This allowed subsequent mobile mapping devices to estimate their physical location by comparing current signal readings to the pre-recorded map (the "fingerprint").13 While highly cost-effective and leveraging existing infrastructure, RSSI is exceptionally susceptible to multipath interference—radio signals constantly bouncing off walls, metallic equipment, and moving personnel—which introduces severe, chaotic fluctuations in signal strength and strictly limits absolute accuracy to several meters.68

Modern Wi-Fi positioning has evolved substantially beyond raw signal strength.69 The IEEE 802.11mc standard introduced Wi-Fi Round Trip Time (RTT), which shifts the technological paradigm from measuring degraded signal strength to capturing precise Time-of-Flight (ToF) measurements.69 By utilizing hardware timestamps to measure the exact nanoseconds it takes for a radio signal to travel between the mobile mapping device and the AP, Wi-Fi RTT can achieve reliable sub-meter accuracy.69 Furthermore, emerging Next-Generation Positioning standards (such as 802.11az) shift the heavy computational burden of localization back to the access point infrastructure, utilizing highly precise phase measurements that complement the performance of more dedicated positioning hardware.69 Advanced enterprise networking systems, such as Aruba Open Locate, now actively expose AP physical location data via open APIs; this allows mobile mapping platforms to autonomously auto-locate the network infrastructure upon entering a building, instantly establishing a local geodetic control network without requiring laborious manual setup.69 Additionally, advanced algorithmic approaches like Wi-Fi SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping) developed at institutions like Stanford University enable a mapping device to efficiently build an internal map of Wi-Fi beacons purely from movement, without requiring any initial configuration or physical fingerprinting walks.71

### **5.2 Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) and Phase-Based Ranging**

Bluetooth Low Energy provides a baseline positioning accuracy spectrum comparable to legacy Wi-Fi (typically 2 to 4 meters using RSSI) but operates at a fraction of the power consumption.68 This extreme energy efficiency makes BLE ideal for deploying highly dense matrices of temporary, battery-powered beacons inside dark mine shafts, subterranean transit tunnels, or complex indoor environments prior to initiating a mapping run.67

The true geodetic value of BLE, however, emerged with the introduction of Angle of Arrival (AoA) and Angle of Departure (AoD) direction-finding capabilities.69 When a mobile mapping receiver equipped with a specialized multi-antenna array receives a continuous radio tone from a fixed BLE beacon, it captures rapid In-Phase and Quadrature (IQ) samples as the signal wave physically crosses the different spatial elements of the antenna array.14 By mathematically measuring the minute phase differences of the radio wave hitting each antenna element, the receiver can calculate the exact relative angle to the beacon, enabling sub-meter absolute accuracy.14 Looking forward, the Bluetooth 6.0 specification introduces LE Channel Sounding, utilizing advanced phase-based ranging combined with round-trip time measurements to promise theoretical centimeter-level precision, though its practical deployment in dynamic environments remains hindered by strict line-of-sight requirements and heavy multipath distortion.69

### **5.3 Ultra-Wideband (UWB) for Micro-Positioning**

When the required spatial tolerance of an indoor DEM drops below ten centimeters, Ultra-Wideband (UWB) is unequivocally the premier RF solution.13 Operating across a massive frequency range (typically 3.1 to 10.6 GHz), UWB transceivers emit incredibly short, low-power radio pulses.13 The exceedingly short temporal duration of these pulses (often in the sub-nanosecond range) makes it mathematically easy for receivers to distinguish the true, direct-path signal from the delayed multipath reflections bouncing off walls, floors, and clutter.13

UWB localization relies primarily on precise Time-of-Flight (ToF) trilateration, measuring the absolute transit time to several fixed anchor nodes to calculate a highly precise 3D spatial coordinate.13 While exceptionally accurate, UWB signals suffer from rapid attenuation over extended distances and are easily absorbed or blocked by dense physical objects (such as concrete pillars or heavy machinery), necessitating a significantly high density of strategically placed anchor nodes throughout the survey area to maintain a continuous line of sight.13

## **6\. Private Terrestrial Constellations and Pseudolites**

To achieve GNSS-level (centimeter) accuracy in localized areas without relying on vulnerable satellites, specialized terrestrial positioning systems, frequently referred to as "pseudolites" (pseudo-satellites), have been engineered. The Locata positioning system exemplifies this advanced architecture, creating a localized terrestrial constellation of robust transceivers that broadcast high-precision positioning and timing signals identical in function to GNSS.2

A critical differentiation of Locata networks is that they do not rely on immensely expensive atomic clocks for inter-node synchronization.2 Instead, they utilize a proprietary time-synchronization technique that perfectly aligns the phase of the transmissions across the entire network to the sub-nanosecond level, creating a coherent local positioning grid.2 In complex geodetic mapping operations, such as continuously monitoring the sheer walls of massive open-pit mines for structural deformation, or mapping steep, deeply forested terrain where sky visibility is highly restricted and GNSS is unreliable, a localized terrestrial constellation guarantees un-jammed, continuous RTK-level accuracy.2 For instance, integrating mobile terrestrial LiDAR scanners or even modern smartphone-based LiDAR systems (which can achieve vertical RMSEs of 0.16 meters) with such high-precision local networks allows for the generation of extremely dense, sub-centimeter point clouds without requiring the surveyor to establish physical ground control via traditional optical total stations.72

## **7\. The Unifying Mathematical Framework: Sensor Fusion for Continuous Trajectory**

Regardless of the specific technology deployed—be it 5G NR, Wi-Fi RTT, eLORAN, or a dense network of UWB anchors—no single non-GNSS system provides the ubiquitous, flawless spatial coverage required for uninterrupted, high-resolution continuous mobile mapping.15 Electromagnetic signals inevitably suffer from transient physical blockages, localized multipath interference, unexpected attenuation, and geometric dilution of precision. The true power of these disparate location systems is unlocked only when they are mathematically synthesized through the rigorous framework of Sensor Fusion.11

### **7.1 Inertial Navigation and the Drift Dilemma**

At the core of virtually any advanced mobile mapping platform is the Inertial Navigation System (INS), typically comprising a sophisticated Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) containing multiaxial accelerometers and precision gyroscopes.10 In subterranean mapping applications, such as utilizing a smartphone for photogrammetric tunnel modeling, the system leverages Pedestrian Dead Reckoning (PDR) or algorithmic integration of the IMU data to continuously estimate relative translation (distance traveled) and rotation (heading) over time.13

The critical, inherent flaw of any INS is that its physical measurement errors are cumulative.13 Minute sensor noise, bias instability, and numerical integration errors result in "drift." Within minutes (or mere seconds, for consumer-grade MEMS sensors), an unaided INS will report a spatial position that diverges exponentially from reality, rendering the resulting DEM completely warped and geodetically useless.11

### **7.2 The Kalman Filter Integration**

To counteract catastrophic inertial drift, the INS is tightly coupled with absolute external measurements—such as a Wi-Fi AoA vector, a 5G TDOA position update, or a UWB range measurement—using advanced recursive algorithms, predominantly the Extended Kalman Filter (EKF), the Unscented Kalman Filter (UKF), or Dual Neural Extended Kalman Filtering.10

The Kalman Filter acts as an optimal, mathematically rigorous state estimator operating in two distinct, continuous phases.13 First, in the *Prediction Phase*, the filter utilizes the high-frequency internal data from the IMU to propagate and predict the current state vector (3D position, velocity, and attitude) of the mapping platform.13 This prediction is smooth and continuous but inherently drifting. Second, in the *Correction Phase*, the filter ingests the asynchronous, lower-frequency, but absolute observations from the external RF location systems (e.g., UWB or 5G).13 By assigning rigorous statistical weights to both the internal prediction and the external measurement based on their known error covariances and signal-to-noise ratios, the Kalman Filter "corrects" the spatial trajectory, effectively pulling the drifting inertial path back to absolute reality.13

For example, an uncrewed aerial vehicle flying through an urban canyon may rely primarily on its internal IMU and visual SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping) for micro-navigation.79 As the visual SLAM algorithm slowly accumulates rigid translational errors (especially in the Z-axis height), an intermittent 5G AOA measurement or a reading from a known BLE beacon provides an absolute spatial anchor.64 The Kalman Filter fuses this anchor data, instantly eliminating the accumulated drift and ensuring the topographic LiDAR point cloud aligns perfectly with the absolute global reference frame.10

### **7.3 Machine Learning and the Future of Location Intelligence**

Traditional deterministic sensor fusion struggles significantly when external RF signals are consistently corrupted by highly dynamic, non-Gaussian noise, such as severe NLOS conditions where the only signal received by the mobile mapper is a scattered reflection bouncing down a tunnel.13 To solve this critical vulnerability, the next generation of DEM positioning systems integrates Machine Learning (ML) directly into the localization pipeline.13

Techniques such as the K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) algorithm are heavily utilized to rapidly match incoming Wi-Fi and BLE signal features against massive pre-recorded fingerprint databases to isolate optimal reference points.13 Furthermore, Naïve Bayes Classifiers (NBC) are deployed to probabilistically detect whether a UWB or Wi-Fi signal is operating in a clear Line-of-Sight condition or if it is obstructed, allowing the fusion engine to dynamically adjust the statistical weight of that specific measurement.13 More complex, deep-learning models, such as Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) recurrent neural networks, are deployed specifically to infer behavioral patterns from time-series inertial data.13 LSTMs proactively predict and mathematically minimize PDR localization errors before they compound into massive trajectory shifts.13

To handle the immense data requirements of these models, Support Vector Machines (SVM) and Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) are frequently employed.13 GANs simulate vast arrays of artificial training data (creating "fake" but statistically accurate multipath scenarios) for the mapping sensors, ensuring that the localization system can adapt to sudden structural changes in the physical environment during a surveying campaign without requiring laborious manual recalibration.13 By successfully fusing geomagnetic field data, optical light sensor measurements, and multiple Wi-Fi signals through Deep Feedforward (DFF) neural networks, modern mobile platforms can achieve mean localization errors below 5 centimeters in complex, highly cluttered indoor testbeds without requiring the deployment of expensive, specialized hardware arrays.80

## **Conclusion**

The generation of an exhaustive, accurate, and geodetically sound Digital Elevation Model is no longer entirely tethered to the availability of the Global Navigation Satellite System. As critical mapping missions push further into deep urban canyons, complex subterranean transit networks, and heavily contested electromagnetic environments where GNSS is actively degraded, geodetic engineers must leverage the full, unified spectrum of terrestrial location systems.

From the immense, concrete-penetrating ground waves of modern eLORAN networks and the high-density spatial geometry of 5G massive MIMO cellular arrays to the micro-positioning precision of UWB pulses and advanced Wi-Fi RTT, a vast and highly capable array of positioning tools is now available. While each technology possesses unique physical limitations regarding spatial range, dimensional output (2D versus 3D), and susceptibility to localized multipath interference, their mathematical integration via Extended Kalman Filtering and advanced deep neural networks ensures the continuous spatial integrity of the mapping platform. Through this sophisticated, multi-modal sensor fusion, the uncrewed aerial vehicles, subterranean robotic rovers, and hydrographic survey vessels of tomorrow will map the Earth's topography with unprecedented resilience, continuity, and geometric precision.
